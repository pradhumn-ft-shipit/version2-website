Five years ago, advisor recruitment conversations were about payout grids, brand recognition, and product access. Those still matter. But something has shifted in what advisors actually ask about when they're evaluating a move.

They ask about transitions.

Specifically: How long does a transition take at your firm? What's your NIGO rejection rate? What technology do you use for repapering? Who manages the operations work, and how quickly do my clients actually get moved over?

Advisors who have survived a painful transition — who watched 90 days of operations chaos erode client relationships and delay their revenue — are not going to repeat that experience voluntarily. They evaluate their next firm's transition infrastructure before they evaluate almost anything else.

This is the opening that growth-focused RIAs and broker-dealers are exploiting.

## The New Reality of Advisor Recruiting

The wealth management industry moves roughly 11,000 advisors between firms annually. That number has grown 16% year over year as the independent channel continues to gain share from wirehouses and regional broker-dealers.

Every one of those advisors is evaluating multiple firms. The firms winning those conversations in 2026 have figured out that operational capability — specifically transition operational capability — is a recruiting differentiator that most competitors can't match.

The proof is simple: almost no firm has a good answer to "what's your average transition timeline?" Most say 60–90 days and try to change the subject. Firms using FastTrackr AI can say 3 weeks, and they can prove it.

## What Advisors Are Asking (And What Firms Can't Answer)

In recruiting conversations with advisors managing $200M–$500M books, the same questions come up:

**"How long does a transition typically take at your firm?"**
Honest answer at most firms: 60–90 days, sometimes longer for complex books. Firms with transition automation: 3–4 weeks.

**"What's your process for managing the paperwork?"**
Most firms: "We have an operations team that handles it." Firms with a real answer: "We use [specific platform] that pre-populates all custodial forms and tracks NIGO resolution automatically."

**"What's your NIGO rejection rate?"**
Most firms: blank stare, or "we don't track that." Firms with automation: a specific, low number they can document.

**"Will my clients actually feel a difference in service quality during the transition?"**
Most firms: reassurances. Firms with a genuine answer: "Our transition platform includes a client communication workflow that keeps every client informed at each step. Most of our advisors report that clients are impressed by how organized the process is."

**"How many transitions have you done, and can I talk to advisors who've been through it?"**
This is the question that separates firms who talk about their transition process from those who've actually built one.

## Building a Transition Technology Pitch That's Credible

The recruiting pitch around technology only works if it's specific and verifiable. Generic claims — "we have great technology," "our platform is easy to use" — don't move advisors. What moves them is specificity backed by evidence.

A credible technology pitch for advisor recruitment includes:

**Specific timeline claims.** "Average transition time at our firm for a $300M book is 22 business days, measured from first client contact to last account transfer completed." Not "a few weeks." A number.

**NIGO rate data.** "Our NIGO rejection rate across all transitions last year was 4.2%. Industry average is 15–20%." If you can't cite this number, you haven't automated enough to know it.

**Reference advisors.** Firms that have built a strong transition track record can offer prospective advisors conversations with previous recruits — advisors who can speak from personal experience about what the process was actually like.

**A demo of the platform.** Show, don't tell. Pulling up the transition dashboard in a recruiting conversation and walking an advisor through what their book migration would look like is more persuasive than any slide deck.

**Transition support staffing.** How many dedicated transition operations people does the firm have? Who is the specific person who would manage the advisor's transition? Advisors want names, not org charts.

## The Economics of Transition Speed for Recruiting

Fast transitions aren't just a quality-of-life benefit. They have direct economic implications for both the firm and the advisor.

For the advisor: every day in transition is a day their clients are partially unsettled. Clients with accounts split between the old and new custodian are more likely to consolidate elsewhere. Advisors who complete transitions in 3 weeks lose less AUM than those who take 90 days — consistently, across all book sizes.

For the recruiting firm: faster transitions mean faster revenue. A $300M book generating 1% in fees produces roughly $8,200 in daily revenue when fully operational. Every day that book is in transition, some portion of that revenue is either delayed or at risk. Cutting the transition from 90 to 21 days recovers roughly 69 days of at-risk revenue — a number that substantially exceeds the cost of the technology platform in the first case.

When recruiting teams present this economics framework to advisors evaluating moves, it reframes the technology conversation. The transition platform isn't a perk. It's a financial instrument that protects the advisor's revenue and client relationships during the most vulnerable period in their career.

## Building the Technology-First Recruiting Package

Firms that want to compete on transition quality need a recruiting package that reflects it. This means:

**A transition process document.** A clear, step-by-step description of what the firm's transition process looks like, with timelines, responsible parties, and technology tools identified at each step. Advisors who've experienced ambiguous transition processes will immediately recognize and value specificity.

**A transition timeline visualization.** Show what a 100-household transition looks like week by week: when client outreach happens, when forms go out, when custodial submissions occur, when transfers complete. Visual timelines are more persuasive than written descriptions.

**Case studies from recent transitions.** Anonymized or named (with permission) examples of specific transitions the firm has run: book size, timeline, NIGO rate, retention rate, notable challenges and how they were resolved.

**Technology demonstrations.** The ability to show the actual transition platform to a prospective advisor — not a screenshot in a deck but a live demo — is a significant differentiator. Most recruiting conversations never get to this level of specificity.

**Dedicated transition support contact.** A named person who will own the advisor's transition end-to-end. In a market where advisors have been burned by "support teams" that turned out to be overwhelmed generalists, a dedicated transition manager is a meaningful commitment.

## Where This Goes Wrong

Technology-first recruiting fails when the technology claim outpaces the actual capability. Advisors talk to each other. If a firm promises 3-week transitions and delivers 90-day ones, the recruiting pipeline dries up — because advisors at the firm are telling their networks the truth.

The technology investment has to come first. The recruiting pitch is downstream of the actual operational capability. Firms that build the transition infrastructure before they lead with it in recruiting conversations have a credible story. Firms that lead with the story before building the infrastructure create a liability.
