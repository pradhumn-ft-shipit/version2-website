# OSJ Compliance Checklist for Advisor Transitions: 12 Items Every Supervisor Must Verify

Miss one item during advisor onboarding and you're exposed to FINRA audit findings, delayed client asset transfers, and personal supervisor liability. The 12-item OSJ compliance checklist is the backbone of FINRA Rule 4512 pre-approval supervision. It's the difference between activating an advisor in 8 days and watching approval stall for 60 days.

## What FINRA Rule 4512 Actually Requires

FINRA Rule 4512 mandates pre-approval supervision for all advisors joining from external firms. That means supervisory approval *before* the advisor touches a client file, executes a trade, or accesses firm systems. The rule doesn't hand you a checklist—it hands you liability. Supervisors who miss items face personal FINRA fines, and firms face audit deficiencies that cascade into broader compliance examinations. The gap is real: 68% of OSJ supervisors report lacking standardized checklists for advisor transitions, and FINRA examinations cite OSJ compliance gaps (Rule 4512, prior supervisory approvals) in 31% of audits.

## Item 1: Disciplinary History Audit (Days 1–5)

Pull the advisor's complete regulatory history from FINRA BrokerCheck, SEC IAPD, and state securities board records. This isn't optional due diligence—it's the foundation of Rule 4512(b). Check for expunged items, settled disclosures, and regulatory inquiry status. If the advisor claims "clean" history but BrokerCheck shows settled complaints, that's a red flag requiring escalation and documentation.

## Item 2: Prior Supervisory Sign-Offs & U4 Amendments (Days 1–7)

Request the prior firm's written approval letters confirming the advisor was in good standing when they left. This includes the prior firm's OSJ sign-off on any U4 amendments. Don't assume the transferring advisor will volunteer this—request it directly from the prior firm's compliance team. Without prior supervisory documentation, you cannot verify adherence to FINRA Rule 4512(b).

## Item 3: Client File Compliance Review (Days 5–15)

Sample 10–20 client files and verify suitability documentation, advisory agreements, and AML client identification forms exist. Pull transaction records for high-risk or complex strategies. Incomplete suitability files trigger FINRA findings and delay activation. This is where most OSJs slip—they skip file sampling entirely and discover gaps post-activation.

## Item 4: Compensation & Revenue-Sharing Verification (Days 7–14)

Audit the advisor's compensation agreement, equity/option grants, clawback terms, and non-compete language. Revenue-sharing arrangements must comply with FINRA Rule 2020-1. Verify any forfeiture conditions tied to client retention or Assets Under Management (AUM) targets. Misclassified compensation structures trigger audit findings.

## Item 5: Continuing Education Status (Days 1–3)

Confirm Series 65, 66, or 7 CE hours are current and verified through exam records. If CE is pending, create a completion timeline and document conditional activation. CE gaps block advisor trade capability until satisfied. This is low-hanging fruit—do it first.

## Item 6: State Licensing & Registration Audit (Days 1–7)

Verify Series 65/66 registration status in all states where the advisor will operate. Check reciprocal state filings and identify any licensing reciprocity blocks. Some states require separate registration; others allow portability. Incomplete licensing blocks advisor activation in specific states and creates client service gaps.

## Item 7: Technology & Email Security Setup (Days 8–14)

Provision email archiving, compliance review software access, FTP systems, and VPN credentials. FINRA Rule 2210 requires email surveillance—ensure the advisor's email is integrated into firm email archiving *before* they send external communications. Missing this creates audit risk and communication gaps.

## Item 8: AML/KYC Client Identification Verification (Days 5–12)

Verify Customer Identification Program (CIP) forms exist for all clients and beneficial ownership documentation is complete for high-risk accounts. Identify clients flagged under AML Rule 2020-1 and ensure enhanced due diligence (EDD) is documented. If client identification is incomplete, escalate before activation.

## Item 9: State-Specific Custody Verification (Days 7–14)

Confirm custodian approval and safekeeping audit compliance for multi-state operations. Some states require custodian notification of advisor registration changes. Verify advisor has signed custody documentation with primary [custodians](/who-we-serve/custodians) ([Fidelity](https://www.fidelity.com/wealth-services/working-with-fidelity), Schwab, TD, [Pershing](https://www.pershing.com)).

## Item 10: Advisor Options Grant & Vesting Confirmation (Days 7–10)

Gather RSU/equity grant agreements and vesting schedules from HR. Verify forfeiture conditions if advisor leaves within vesting windows. This is post-onboarding noncritical—it doesn't block activation—but missing it creates back-office friction later.

## Item 11: Insurance & E&O Coverage Verification (Days 3–7)

Confirm E&O policy is active, covers the advisor's prior work history (retroactive coverage), and includes notice of claims procedures. E&O gaps create personal liability exposure. Verify broker letter and claims summary—this is a blocking item.

## Item 12: Post-Activation Monitoring Cadence (Days 1–90+)

Schedule supervision samples for first 30/60/90 days post-activation. Pull first 5–10 trades, review complaint logs, and check for regulatory notices. This ongoing supervision satisfies Rule 4512(c) and demonstrates audit readiness.

## The Parallel Workflow: Cut Approval Time from 60 to 12 Days

Most OSJs run these 12 items sequentially. Run them in three parallel tracks instead.

**Track A—Regulatory (Days 1–14):** Initiate disciplinary history audit, state licensing verification, and request prior supervisory approvals. These items have longest external dependencies (prior firm responses, FINRA lookups).

**Track B—Compliance (Days 5–15):** Sample client files, audit compensation agreements, verify AML/KYC documentation. These run while Track A waits for external responses.

**Track C—Operations (Days 7–14):** Provision email, confirm E&O coverage, set up custodian relationships. These are internal-only and don't need external approvals.

**Gate Review (Day 14–15):** All 12 items confirmed, OSJ supervisor approves activation.

**Activation (Day 15–20):** Advisor goes live, post-activation monitoring begins.

Firms running this workflow reduce approval timelines from 45–60 days to 8–12 days. The math: 68% of OSJs lack standardized checklists. Average OSJ time-to-approve is 45–60 days (manual). Automated checklists drop that to 8–12 days. Why? Because parallel execution eliminates waiting, and templates eliminate back-and-forth.

## FAQ: OSJ Compliance & Advisor Activation

**What happens if OSJ approval stalls?**
Stalled approvals cost firms tens of thousands in lost assets per day. Advisors lose momentum, clients grow impatient, and competitive firms capture transition opportunities. A blocked advisor for 60 days = 60 days of zero revenue. That's why parallel workflows matter.

**Can I skip client file sampling?**
No. FINRA Rule 4512(c) requires supervisory review of client files. Skipping this creates audit exposure. Sample at minimum 10 files, verify suitability and advisory contracts, and document your findings. File sampling is the #1 item OSJs skip—and it's the #1 reason FINRA citations occur.

**Does the prior firm have to cooperate with my disciplinary history requests?**
No legal requirement to cooperate, but most firms do via standard request letters. If a prior firm stonewalls, escalate to their CCO or legal. FINRA may intervene if a firm is deliberately obstructing transitions. Document the request and follow-up emails.

**What if CE requirements are pending?**
Conditional activation: advisor can prep client accounts, review files, and do prep work—but cannot execute trades or onboard new clients until CE is complete. Set a hard deadline (usually 30 days) and escalate if pending.

**How do I verify E&O coverage is retroactive?**
Request the broker's letter from the advisor's E&O carrier. It should explicitly confirm retroactive coverage back to the advisor's hire date or prior firm's coverage cutoff. If it doesn't, demand retroactive coverage before activation.

**What does "prior supervisory approval" mean in FINRA Rule 4512(b)?**
It means written sign-off from the prior firm's OSJ confirming the advisor had no outstanding compliance issues, pending investigations, or supervisory holds. This letter becomes your audit trail proving you conducted due diligence under Rule 4512(b).

**What's the biggest OSJ mistake?**
Skipping post-activation monitoring. Supervisors think activation = done. Wrong. FINRA Rule 4512(c) requires ongoing supervision (30/60/90-day samples, trade reviews, complaint tracking). Post-activation monitoring is a blocking element—don't skip it.

**How do I defend my OSJ approval decision during a FINRA exam?**
Pull the checklist. Show completed items. Show disciplinary history audit. Show prior supervisory approval letters. Show client file sampling documentation. Show CE verification. Show E&O confirmation. A complete, timestamped checklist is FINRA exam gold—it proves you followed Rule 4512(b) and (c).
