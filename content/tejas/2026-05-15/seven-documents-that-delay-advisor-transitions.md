After working through thousands of advisor transitions, the bottlenecks stop being surprising. The same seven documents cause the same delays, in the same order, across firms of every size.

This isn't a people problem. Operations teams are smart, experienced, and trying hard. The delays happen because these documents are structurally difficult — they require information that's hard to gather, have validation rules that aren't intuitive, or depend on custodian-specific requirements that change without notice.

Understanding exactly where transitions break down is the first step to fixing them.

---

## 1. Account Transfer Authorization (ATA) Forms

The ATA is the workhorse of any custodial transfer. It's also the most frequently rejected document in a transition.

The problems are predictable: account titles that don't match the receiving firm's records exactly, outdated address information, missing co-account holder signatures. [Custodians](/who-we-serve/custodians) have zero tolerance for discrepancies — a single character difference between the account title at Firm A and the account title at Firm B triggers rejection.

The fix is pre-validation before submission: cross-referencing client data against custodian records before the form is ever generated, flagging mismatches automatically, and surfacing corrections before the first NIGO ever happens.

## 2. New Account Application (NAA) Forms

Every custodian has its own NAA form, and most have updated their requirements in the last 18 months. The problem isn't that ops teams don't know the requirements — it's that requirements change, and the old version of the form template is still floating around.

Firms running manual repapering workflows often discover they're working from outdated templates only after the first batch of rejections comes back. At that point, they've lost a week and have to regenerate every form.

Version control for form templates — automatically updated when custodians publish new requirements — eliminates this failure mode entirely.

## 3. Client Data Verification Forms

Custodians require updated client data at transfer: current address, employment status, investment objectives, risk tolerance. For an advisor with a 400-household book, collecting this information manually is a multi-week project.

The structural problem is that the advisor has to reach every client, many of whom don't respond quickly. The longer this takes, the longer the entire transition waits.

Automated data collection — structured intake that pulls from existing records, pre-fills what can be verified, and prompts clients digitally for only the data that's genuinely missing — can compress this step from weeks to days.

## 4. W-9 / Taxpayer Identification Forms

W-9s seem simple. They're not, in practice.

For business accounts, trust accounts, and joint accounts, the beneficial ownership questions and certification signatures can get complicated. Business structures change. Trustees die. Joint account holders need to be individually verified.

The most common W-9 failure is submitting for an entity that has changed its legal structure since the original account was opened — and the advisor doesn't know because the client never mentioned it.

A clean intake process that explicitly asks clients to confirm current entity structure before form generation catches these before submission, not after.

## 5. Beneficiary Designation Forms

Beneficiary forms are submitted late and rejected often. They're submitted late because advisors frequently assume clients have their beneficiary designations correct — and clients assume the advisor knows. They're rejected often because the forms require specific legal names and relationships that don't always match what's on file.

More importantly: the window around a transition is a high-stakes time to get beneficiary designations right. Clients are paying attention to their accounts. If a designation is wrong and a client discovers it during a transition, the trust damage is significant.

Building beneficiary verification into the standard transition intake — rather than treating it as an afterthought — catches discrepancies early and builds advisor credibility.

## 6. Regulatory Disclosure Forms (Form ADV, Reg BI)

For RIA transitions and situations where advisors change broker-dealer affiliation, regulatory disclosure forms create compliance bottlenecks. Form ADV Part 2 needs to be updated and delivered to each client within the regulatory window. Reg BI best interest disclosure obligations apply to the new firm.

These forms aren't hard to generate, but they have strict delivery and acknowledgment requirements. Tracking delivery and acknowledgment across 400 households manually — with a compliance deadline — is exactly the kind of task that gets rushed, makes errors, and creates audit exposure.

Automated delivery tracking with acknowledgment capture turns a compliance liability into a documented audit trail.

## 7. Trust and Estate Account Documentation

Complex account types — irrevocable trusts, estate accounts, UTMA/UGMA accounts, accounts with power of attorney — require additional documentation that varies by custodian and by account type.

These are the accounts that cause transitions to stall in the final stretch. The simple accounts are done. The standard forms are filed. Then there are 15 trust accounts still sitting in limbo because the documentation requirements are different and no one has had time to address them.

Complex account types should be identified at the start of the transition, not at the end. Flagging them early means they're worked in parallel with the standard accounts rather than as a final cleanup after everything else is done.

---

## The Pattern Behind the Delays

Every one of these seven documents has the same underlying issue: information required for submission is incomplete, outdated, or incorrect at the time it's needed.

The traditional solution is for ops teams to chase down the right information as each problem surfaces. That's reactive. It keeps transitions slow.

The proactive solution is to gather, validate, and verify all required information before a single form is generated — so that when forms are created, they're ready to submit and ready to clear.

That shift, from reactive to proactive, is what separates a 90-day transition from a 3-week transition.
