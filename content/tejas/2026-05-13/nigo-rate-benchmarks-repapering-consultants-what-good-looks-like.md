Most repapering consultants know when their NIGO rate is too high. They feel it — in the constant resubmissions, in the client calls asking where their account is, in the midnight emails from advisors who were promised a clean transition.

But what's the actual benchmark? What does "good" look like?

Almost no one publishes this number. Docupace doesn't. Skience doesn't. Manual operations teams don't track it formally enough to report it. This article does.

## What Is a NIGO and Why It Matters More Than You Think

NIGO stands for Not In Good Order — a form rejected by a custodian because a field was incorrect, a signature was missing, or data didn't match the custodian's records.

Each NIGO doesn't just create an inconvenience. It resets the clock on that account. The form goes back. New signatures may be required. The custodian rejects submission, you correct and resubmit, and that account now has a longer timeline than every other account in the batch.

For a single advisor moving 500 client accounts, a 20% NIGO rate means 100 accounts cycling through rejection and resubmission — any one of which is an open window for the advisor's former firm to reach out to that client during the delay.

At 15-20 simultaneous transitions, that math compounds into something that no manual team can manage with integrity.

## Why Each Custodian Has Different Rules (And Why That Matters Most)

The single biggest driver of NIGOs is custodian-specific form requirements that manual operations teams cannot keep current with.

Fidelity's requirements differ from Schwab's. Schwab's differ from Pershing's. Each custodian has specific field formats, signature requirements, account titling conventions, and documentation standards. Those requirements change periodically. A manual ops team tracking custodian-specific rules across multiple custodians simultaneously is always one update behind.

True pre-validation means validating each form against the target custodian's current, specific rules before submission — not running a generic "is this field filled in?" check. The difference between a 2% NIGO rate and a 15% NIGO rate is almost entirely this single capability.

If your current platform claims pre-validation, ask them: "Can you show me Schwab's specific field validation rules in your system, and how they differ from Fidelity's?" If they can't answer that question with specifics, they're running a generic check.

## What FINRA Expects From Your Audit Trail in 2026

NIGO management isn't just an operations problem — it's a compliance problem.

FINRA's 2026 Annual Regulatory Oversight Report flagged recordkeeping deficiencies more than 50 times. Examiners are looking at how firms document the full transition cycle: who submitted, what was submitted, what was rejected, why it was rejected, how it was resolved, and when it was resubmitted. That's the audit trail for a NIGO.

Manual systems produce audit trails as a byproduct of the ops team's memory and spreadsheet discipline. Automated systems produce audit trails as a byproduct of every action being logged. The difference matters when an examiner asks you to reconstruct Account X's full history in 30 minutes.

Smaller broker-dealers must also comply with enhanced Regulation S-P data safeguard requirements by June 3, 2026 — including how client PII is handled during the transition process itself.

## The 5-Step NIGO Prevention Checklist for Repapering Consultants

If you're not yet using a platform with purpose-built pre-validation, you can reduce your NIGO rate manually with these steps — though they won't get you to under 2% without the automation layer.

**Step 1: Pre-validate each form against the target custodian's current requirements**

Download the latest submission guides from each custodian you're working with. Build a checklist for each custodian's specific field requirements. Check every form against it before submission. Update the checklist when custodians announce changes.

**Step 2: Use real-time status tracking to catch pending items before they become rejected items**

Build a tracking system (even a carefully maintained spreadsheet) that shows you every account by status: data collection, form generation, pending signatures, submitted, in review, completed. Items sitting in "pending signatures" for more than 48 hours need a follow-up trigger.

**Step 3: Separate multi-custodian transitions into custodian-specific batches**

Don't mix Fidelity and Schwab accounts in the same workflow pass. Run each custodian's requirements as a separate checklist pass to avoid cross-contamination errors.

**Step 4: Automate signature reminders**

Most late NIGOs happen because clients didn't sign. Don't rely on advisors to follow up. Build a reminder schedule — 48 hours after form delivery, 24 hours before deadline — and run it systematically.

**Step 5: Build your audit trail from day one, not after the fact**

Every action you take on every account needs a timestamp and a log entry before you move to the next action. The discipline of logging as you go is what makes your audit trail FINRA-ready rather than reconstructed.

## The Cost of Your Current NIGO Rate

Here's the calculation most repapering consultants have never actually run.

Take your average NIGO rate (estimate if you don't track it). Multiply by your average number of accounts per transition. Multiply by the average number of days a NIGO adds to that account's timeline. Multiply by the number of simultaneous transitions you're running.

That's the total at-risk timeline extension your current NIGO rate is producing.

For a consultant running 15 simultaneous transitions at a 20% NIGO rate, with 500 accounts each and each NIGO adding 7 days:
- 15 transitions × 500 accounts × 20% = 1,500 accounts generating NIGOs
- 1,500 NIGOs × 7 days = 10,500 account-days of extended timeline
- Spread across 15 advisors: each advisor's transition is an average of 700 days slower in aggregate account completions

If your NIGO rate is above 10%, your clients are waiting weeks longer than they need to. And every extra week is a week where their relationship with the transition experience — and with you — is being tested.

The benchmark is under 2%. That's achievable with the right platform. That's where the industry is going.
