# Board Deck: Data Points and Case Studies for Investing in Transition Tech

The hardest part of getting transition technology approved at a broker-dealer or RIA isn't the operational case — it's translating the operational case into a board-ready financial narrative. Operations teams are fluent in NIGO rates and AUM retention. Boards are fluent in capital allocation, recruiting yield, and competitive positioning. The bridge between the two languages is where most transition technology investment cases either get approved or get sent back for "more analysis."

This is the board deck framework that bridges them. It's structured around the questions a board will actually ask, in the order they'll ask them, with the data points and case studies that produce confident decisions rather than further deliberation.

## Slide 1: the strategic question

The first slide of the board deck isn't about technology. It's about strategic positioning. The question to answer: "Why are we evaluating this investment now?"

The honest answer at most BDs in 2026 is that recruiting yields have softened because advisors are increasingly choosing firms with better transition capabilities. According to [WealthManagement.com data](https://www.wealthmanagement.com/recruiting/advisor-movement-soared-16-in-2025), 11,172 advisors changed firms in 2025 — up 16% from 2024. The firms winning that recruiting volume are the ones with credibility on transition timeline, NIGO rates, and AUM retention. Firms without that credibility are losing recruits without ever knowing it — the advisor never starts the conversation because the operational reputation precedes them.

The strategic frame for slide 1 is therefore: "Our recruiting capability is bottlenecked by our transition capability. We can either accept declining recruiting yields or invest in the operational platform that produces competitive transition capability."

This frame works because it connects the operational investment to a board-level concern — recruiting yield — that the board already cares about. Without that connection, transition technology investment looks like an operations expense rather than a strategic move.

## Slide 2: the market data

The board needs data that establishes the size and growth of the opportunity. Three categories of data work well.

**Industry transition volume.** The 11,172 advisor moves in 2025, the 16% year-over-year growth, the 54 teams managing over $1 billion in AUM that moved in 2025 alone. These data points establish that this is a growing market segment, not a niche concern.

**Cost of transition failure.** The $19 billion the industry loses annually to advisor transitions — most of it from accounts that aren't formally lost but are operationally diluted during slow transitions. This number frames the upside of operational improvement.

**Competitive performance gaps.** The performance differential between best-in-class transition platforms and industry-average operations: 21-day average timelines versus 75-day industry averages, sub-5% NIGO rates versus 15-30% industry averages, 95%+ AUM retention versus 85-90% averages. These gaps are the basis for the ROI math on later slides.

The data has to be sourced. Boards will discount any data point that doesn't have a credible source. The good news is that this data is widely covered by industry publications and consulting firms — sourcing isn't difficult, but it has to be done.

## Slide 3: the ROI math

The ROI slide is what gets the board comfortable with the dollar commitment. The math should be transparent and conservative.

**Revenue side: incremental recruiting yield.** If the firm's transition capability shifts from "industry average" to "competitive with best-in-class," what's the incremental advisor recruitment per year? A reasonable conservative estimate for a mid-sized BD is 5-10 additional advisor moves per year. At an average $200M book and 0.8% fee, each additional advisor represents $1.6M in annual revenue. Five additional advisors per year is $8M in annual revenue at maturity (year 3+).

**Revenue side: AUM retention improvement.** If AUM retention through transitions improves from 88% to 96% (industry average to best-in-class), what does that mean in dollar terms? For a firm processing 30 transitions per year averaging $200M in AUM, an 8 percentage point retention improvement is $480M in retained AUM cumulative. At 0.8% fee, that's $3.8M in annual revenue at the new steady state.

**Cost side: platform investment.** Year 1: $400K-700K licensing plus implementation. Year 2+: $500K-1M ongoing. Operational efficiency in the transition team typically reduces headcount needs by 5-10 people, which can be redeployed to other operational priorities rather than terminated.

**Net ROI.** Annual revenue uplift in year 3: $8M from recruiting plus $3.8M from retention = $11.8M. Annual platform cost: $1M. Net: $10.8M annual at maturity, with payback in year 1.

This ROI math is conservative — it doesn't include downstream benefits like improved compliance posture, reduced operational risk, or the long-term value of being the firm advisors choose first. Even with the conservative assumptions, the math is compelling.

## Slide 4: case studies

Boards make decisions on data, but they remember case studies. The deck should include 2-3 specific case studies of firms that have implemented transition technology and the outcomes they achieved.

**Case study structure:** The firm (anonymized or named with permission), the operational situation pre-implementation, the investment decision, the implementation timeline, the operational outcomes, and the strategic outcomes. Each case study should be a single slide with the key numbers.

The case study has to be specific. "Firm A reduced transition timelines significantly" is forgettable. "Firm A reduced average transition timeline from 87 days to 28 days, NIGO rates from 22% to 4%, and AUM retention from 91% to 97% over the first 18 months — driving $14M in cumulative additional revenue versus the historical baseline" is the level of specificity boards remember.

Case studies are also where the recruiting argument lands most concretely. "Firm B's recruiting yield in year 2 of platform deployment was 31% higher than the trailing 5-year average, with recruited advisors specifically citing the firm's transition capability in their decision rationale." That's a data point a board can act on.

For BD executives building case study material, the firms most willing to share data are the ones who have implemented in the past 18-24 months. Older implementations are often less relevant because both the technology and the market have evolved meaningfully.

## Slide 5: risk and mitigation

Boards always ask about risk. The deck should preempt the questions rather than wait for them.

**Risk 1: implementation failure.** What if the platform doesn't deliver the promised outcomes? Mitigation: structure the contract with SLAs and breach credits, set explicit operational milestones, and build in evaluation gates at 90, 180, and 270 days where the firm can exit if performance is below threshold.

**Risk 2: vendor lock-in.** What if we depend on the platform and the vendor performance deteriorates? Mitigation: contractual data portability requirements, ability to migrate to alternatives if vendor SLAs are missed, and a structured annual review of vendor performance against alternatives.

**Risk 3: organizational change resistance.** What if the operations team doesn't adopt the platform effectively? Mitigation: explicit change management plan, executive sponsorship, dedicated implementation resources, and a defined timeline for cultural adoption.

**Risk 4: competitive response.** What if competitors also implement and the advantage is short-lived? Mitigation: the advantage is meaningful for the 18-24 month window most competitors will need to evaluate and implement, and durable advantages come from operational execution beyond the technology itself.

Acknowledging these risks explicitly demonstrates that the team has thought through the decision. Boards are far more comfortable with investments where risks are named and mitigated than with investments where risks are minimized or denied.

## Slide 6: implementation plan and decision points

The final substantive slide is the implementation plan with explicit decision points. The board wants to know what they're approving and when they'll get to revisit the decision.

**Phase 1: vendor selection (0-90 days).** RFP process, vendor evaluation against the criteria framework, contract negotiation, and final selection. Board update at completion.

**Phase 2: implementation (90-180 days).** Platform provisioning, integration with internal systems, staff training, dress rehearsal on a pilot transition. Board update at completion.

**Phase 3: production rollout (180-360 days).** Initial production transitions, performance measurement against SLAs, operational refinement based on early learnings. Board update at 270 days.

**Phase 4: scale and optimization (year 2).** Full transition volume on the platform, ongoing optimization, vendor relationship management. Board review at year-end and annually thereafter.

The phased structure with explicit decision points gives the board confidence that the investment is being managed actively and they'll have opportunities to course-correct if outcomes don't match expectations.

## Slide 7: the ask

The final slide is the explicit ask. "We are requesting board approval for: $X in year 1 platform investment, $Y in annual ongoing investment, and an implementation timeline of Z months culminating in production deployment by [date]. Expected outcomes: [recruiting yield improvement, AUM retention improvement, operational efficiency]. Evaluation gates: [SLA milestones at 90, 180, 270 days]."

The clearest asks get the clearest answers. Boards approve specific numbers tied to specific outcomes much more reliably than they approve general directional commitments.

## The supporting data appendix

The deck itself should be 7-8 slides. The supporting data should be in an appendix that the board can reference if they want depth, but doesn't clutter the main narrative.

Appendix material that's useful: detailed ROI math with sensitivity analysis, vendor comparison spreadsheet, reference customer interview notes, security and compliance documentation, contract term summary, and the operational metrics baseline that defines pre-investment performance.

Most board members will not read the appendix. The ones who do are typically the audit committee members, who want to validate the rigor of the analysis. The appendix is for them, and it's worth having even if 80% of the board never opens it.

## What boards actually decide on

The pattern across boards that approve transition technology investments quickly versus boards that defer is consistent. Quick approvals happen when the deck connects the investment to a strategic concern the board already has — typically recruiting yield or competitive positioning. Deferrals happen when the deck reads as an operational expense without a clear strategic frame.

For BD executives building this deck, the work of finding the strategic frame is more important than the work of perfecting the operational details. A deck with a strong strategic frame and reasonable operational support will outperform a deck with perfect operational analysis and weak strategic positioning.

For the operations team supporting the deck, the right collaboration model is to provide the operational baseline data and outcomes targets, then let the executive presenting the deck shape the strategic narrative. The deck reflects executive judgment about what the board cares about, informed by operational reality. That collaboration is what produces the deck that actually gets approved.

## Frequently asked questions

**How long should a board deck for transition technology investment be?**

7-8 substantive slides with a supporting appendix. Boards typically allocate 20-30 minutes for an investment of this size, which translates to 7-8 slides at 2-3 minutes each. Decks longer than 12 substantive slides often signal a presenter who doesn't understand what the board needs to decide on.

**What level of ROI math should be in the deck versus the appendix?**

The deck should include the bottom-line ROI summary with clearly stated assumptions. The detailed sensitivity analysis, line-by-line cost breakdowns, and scenario modeling belong in the appendix. Board members who want depth will find it; board members who want the summary will get it cleanly.

**Should the deck name specific vendors being evaluated?**

Generally yes, especially in the case studies and vendor comparison sections. Naming specific vendors demonstrates that the team has done concrete diligence and isn't making a generic case. The contracting and final selection should remain board-approved, but the deck can identify the leading candidates.

**How do you handle the recruiting yield assumption when there's no historical baseline?**

Conservatively. If the firm doesn't have historical data on recruiting yield improvements from operational investments, use industry benchmarks from comparable firms and apply a discount factor to reflect the uncertainty. A 25-40% discount on benchmark numbers produces ROI math that holds up to board scrutiny.

**What case study sources are most credible to boards?**

Anonymized case studies with named firm characteristics (size, segment, geography) but anonymized names are usually most credible, because they balance specificity with the reality that firms rarely share named performance data publicly. If a firm has given permission to be named, that's even stronger — but it's rare in this industry.

**How should the deck handle the question of competitive response?**

Acknowledge that competitors will eventually invest in similar capabilities, but frame the window of advantage as 18-24 months — long enough to produce material recruiting and retention outcomes. Durable advantage comes from operational execution beyond the technology, including the firm's specific custodial relationships, compliance capabilities, and recruiting infrastructure.
