# Per-Transition Pricing for Consulting Firms (vs Per-Seat SaaS)

The default commercial structure for enterprise software is per-seat licensing. Pay per user, scale up as your team grows, predictable budget impact. For most software categories, per-seat works because the value of the software correlates with the number of users — more salespeople using a CRM, more engineers using a developer tool. The [pricing](/pricing) model and the value delivered scale together.

Transition technology is different. The value of the platform correlates with the number of transitions processed, not the number of users sitting at the platform. A 6-person team running 200 transitions a year extracts dramatically more value than a 12-person team running 80 transitions per year. Per-seat licensing prices these two situations identically. Per-transition pricing prices them in proportion to value.

For transition consulting firms specifically, the difference between per-seat and per-transition pricing can change the unit economics of the consulting business in fundamental ways. This is the math, the contract structures, and the practical considerations for consulting firm partners weighing the two models.

## Why per-seat pricing breaks for consulting firms

Per-seat SaaS pricing creates three problems specifically for transition consulting firms.

**Problem 1: pricing decoupled from output.** A consulting firm doesn't sell seats. It sells completed transitions. When the underlying technology costs are priced by seats, the consulting firm has to mark up enough on each transition to cover the seat cost amortized across an uncertain volume. If volume comes in higher than expected, the firm captures the upside. If volume comes in lower, the firm loses money on every transition because the seat cost is fixed.

**Problem 2: misalignment with team scaling.** Consulting firms scale by adding partners and senior staff who supervise more junior teams. The traditional firm structure has 1 partner per 4-6 staff. Under per-seat pricing, adding the team scales the platform cost linearly even though the partner's revenue contribution is non-linear. The economics get worse as the firm grows, not better.

**Problem 3: difficulty in client billing alignment.** Most consulting firms bill clients on a per-transition basis — flat fee per transition processed. When the underlying technology cost is per-seat, the firm is taking on the conversion risk between the two pricing models. If utilization is below 100%, the per-seat cost amortized per transition is higher than expected, eating into margins.

These three problems are why mature transition consulting firms increasingly push back on per-seat licensing and request per-transition or volume-based commercial structures.

## What per-transition pricing actually looks like

Per-transition pricing structures vary, but the common patterns share a few characteristics.

**Pattern 1: pure per-transition.** The consulting firm pays a fixed fee per transition processed on the platform. Typical range: $300-1500 per transition depending on the platform's positioning. Volume discounts apply at tiered thresholds — $500 per transition for the first 100 per year, $400 per transition for 100-300, $300 per transition above 300. The firm has zero technology cost in months with no transitions, full value in months with high volume.

**Pattern 2: minimum commitment plus per-transition overage.** The firm commits to a minimum annual transition volume at a fixed total fee, then pays per-transition above the threshold. Typical structure: $100K minimum commitment covering up to 200 transitions per year, $300 per transition for transitions 201-500, $250 per transition above 500. This gives the firm budget predictability while preserving variable cost upside on volume growth.

**Pattern 3: shared revenue.** The vendor charges a percentage of the consulting firm's revenue per transition, typically 5-15% depending on the platform's value contribution. This model aligns vendor and firm incentives most directly — the vendor's revenue scales with the consulting firm's revenue. It works best when both parties have transparent revenue per transition data.

**Pattern 4: hybrid platform fee plus per-transition.** A baseline platform fee (typically $30K-100K annually) covers core infrastructure access, plus a smaller per-transition fee (typically $100-400) for variable costs like custodial submissions, document processing, and storage. This pattern matches the actual cost structure of running a transition platform.

The right pattern depends on the firm's volume profile, growth rate, and predictability of demand. Firms with consistent year-over-year volume often prefer pattern 2; firms with rapid growth or seasonal demand often prefer pattern 1 or 3.

## The volume break-even math

The break-even math between per-seat and per-transition is straightforward but worth working through explicitly for any consulting firm partner considering the question.

**Sample scenario: a 12-person transition consulting team.**

Under per-seat pricing at $25K per seat per year: $300K annual platform cost.

Under per-transition pricing at $500 per transition with no minimums: break-even at 600 transitions per year (300,000 / 500). Below 600 transitions, per-transition is cheaper. Above 600, per-seat is cheaper.

For a 12-person team, 600 transitions per year is approximately 50 transitions per team member per year — which is realistic for a high-performing team. The break-even is right around the firm's mature operating volume.

**Why per-transition still typically wins for consulting firms.** Even when the break-even math is close, per-transition wins for three reasons. First, it eliminates the conversion risk between seat costs and per-transition revenue. Second, it scales down naturally in low-volume periods rather than locking in costs that have to be recovered. Third, it aligns with client billing models, simplifying internal accounting.

The exception: very high-volume firms doing 1000+ transitions per year with consistent month-over-month demand might find that per-seat licensing produces lower aggregate costs. For those firms, the right answer is often to negotiate a hybrid that captures the predictability of per-seat with the alignment of per-transition.

## Contract structures that support the model

The commercial model is one component; the contract structure that supports it is equally important. Consulting firms negotiating per-transition arrangements should focus on five clauses.

**Clause 1: clear definition of "transition."** When does a transition start (account opening at the new firm, first form submission, custodial confirmation)? When does it end (last form submitted, last custodial confirmation, 90 days after first submission)? Vague definitions lead to billing disputes; precise definitions prevent them.

**Clause 2: volume measurement and reporting cadence.** How is transition volume measured? On what cadence is billing calculated? What happens when volume measurement disputes arise? The contract should specify monthly reporting with quarterly true-ups, plus a defined dispute resolution path.

**Clause 3: minimum guarantees and overage rates.** If the contract has minimums (most do), what's the consequence of being below the minimum? What's the rate above the minimum? Are there volume thresholds where the rate steps down? The economics of the deal depend on these specifics.

**Clause 4: term length and price escalation.** Most per-transition contracts run 2-3 years with annual price escalation clauses. The escalation rate (typically 3-5% annually) should be specified, and the firm should negotiate caps to prevent unexpected step-changes.

**Clause 5: exit terms and data portability.** What happens at contract end? Can the firm migrate to an alternative platform? What's the cooperation requirement during migration? Strong exit terms preserve the firm's strategic optionality regardless of how the relationship evolves.

These five clauses cover roughly 80% of the commercial value at stake in a per-transition contract. Firms that don't negotiate them deliberately often discover years later that the contract terms aren't aligned with how they actually operate.

## When per-seat is still the right choice

There are situations where per-seat licensing is actually the better commercial model for a consulting firm. Worth naming them explicitly.

**Situation 1: very high steady-state volume.** A firm doing 1500+ transitions per year with year-over-year consistency may find that per-seat licensing at enterprise volumes produces lower aggregate costs than per-transition pricing. The trade-off is reduced flexibility in down years.

**Situation 2: predictable budget requirements.** Some consulting firms have backers (PE sponsors, lender covenants) who require predictable operating costs. Per-seat licensing provides budget predictability that per-transition pricing doesn't.

**Situation 3: rapid team growth ahead of transition volume.** If the firm is hiring aggressively ahead of an expected volume ramp, per-seat pricing during the ramp can be cheaper than per-transition pricing on the eventual high volume. This is a temporary arbitrage situation that usually resolves over 12-24 months.

**Situation 4: secondary use cases.** If the consulting firm uses the platform for activities beyond just transitions — internal training, prospect demonstrations, scenario modeling — per-seat licensing covers these activities without additional per-transition charges.

Even in these situations, the right answer is often a hybrid model that captures the benefits of both — a baseline per-seat fee covering steady-state usage plus per-transition pricing for variable workload. Vendors who can structure these hybrid models well are more likely to win enterprise consulting firm business.

## What to expect in vendor negotiations

Consulting firms that have negotiated per-transition pricing with multiple vendors describe a consistent pattern in how the conversations evolve.

The initial vendor proposal is almost always per-seat. The consulting firm has to explicitly request per-transition pricing. The vendor's response depends on their commercial maturity: mature vendors have a per-transition pricing book ready to present; less mature vendors push back with "we don't do that" or offer artificially high per-transition rates designed to drive the firm back toward per-seat.

The negotiation typically takes 2-4 rounds. The first round is about establishing whether per-transition is on the table at all. The second round is about getting to credible per-transition rates. The third and fourth rounds are about volume tiers, contract terms, and the specific clauses above.

Firms that approach this negotiation with concrete volume data from their existing operations get to better commercial terms than firms that approach it with projections. The vendor's pricing is calibrated against their cost structure, which is much more responsive to actual volume than to projected volume.

For partners at consulting firms approaching this negotiation, the most useful preparation is a year of clean transition volume data broken down by month, by transition complexity, and by client type. That data is the foundation for an informed commercial conversation that produces a fair deal for both sides.

## Frequently asked questions

**What's the typical per-transition price range for transition technology platforms?**

$300-$1,500 per transition depending on the platform's positioning, capability set, and the consulting firm's negotiating leverage. Higher-end platforms with strong custodial integrations, mature compliance support, and dedicated implementation resources typically price in the $700-1,200 range. Lower-end form-generation tools price in the $300-500 range.

**At what transition volume does per-seat licensing become cheaper than per-transition pricing?**

Highly dependent on the specific rates being compared, but a useful rule of thumb: per-seat licensing becomes cheaper around 50-80 transitions per seat per year. Below that throughput, per-transition pricing is typically lower. Above it, the math can favor per-seat depending on the structure.

**Should consulting firms negotiate hybrid commercial structures?**

Yes, increasingly. Hybrid structures — a baseline platform fee plus per-transition pricing for variable cost — match the actual cost structure of transition platforms and produce more sustainable commercial relationships. Mature vendors offer hybrid structures as a standard option for enterprise consulting firms.

**How important is the definition of "transition" in a per-transition contract?**

Critical. Vague definitions lead to billing disputes that can damage the vendor relationship. The contract should specify when a transition starts (typically at account opening or first form submission), when it ends (typically at custodial confirmation of the last account or at a defined time threshold), and how partial transitions or failed transitions are billed.

**What's the typical contract length for per-transition agreements?**

2-3 years with annual price escalation clauses is standard. Some vendors offer longer terms (5 years) with stronger price commitments; shorter terms (1 year) are unusual at enterprise scale. The 2-3 year window aligns with most consulting firms' planning horizons while giving the vendor enough commercial stability to price aggressively.

**Can a consulting firm switch from per-seat to per-transition mid-contract?**

Usually only at contract renewal. Mid-contract changes typically require significant commercial negotiation and aren't standard. The right time to negotiate the commercial model is at initial contract signature or at renewal — not during active deployment. Firms expecting to change models should plan for the renewal cycle.
