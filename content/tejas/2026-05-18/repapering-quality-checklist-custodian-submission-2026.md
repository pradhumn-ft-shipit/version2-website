The most expensive NIGO rejection is the one that happens after submission. The cheapest is the one caught before the form leaves the building.

Pre-submission quality review is the operational discipline that separates firms with 2–4% NIGO rates from firms with 60% NIGO rates. It's not a complex process — it's a consistent one. The same errors that generate rejections appear on nearly every transition that skips this step.

This checklist covers what best-practice ops teams verify before every custodian submission packet goes out the door.

## Why Pre-Submission Review Matters More Than Post-Rejection Correction

The reactive approach to NIGO management is to catch rejections when they arrive from the custodian and correct them quickly. This approach is functionally correct — rejections are identified and corrected — but it misunderstands the real cost of the error.

A NIGO rejection that arrives from the custodian 5–7 business days after submission means the entire processing window restarts. For a standard ACATS transfer (5–7 business days from clean submission), one rejection adds 10–14 business days to the timeline. For a 200-account book with a 60% rejection rate, 120 rejections each adding 10+ days creates a timeline that bears no resemblance to the committed delivery date.

The math of pre-submission review is simple: 30 minutes of validation before submission prevents 10+ days of processing delay. The ROI on that 30 minutes is the most favorable ratio in all of transition operations.

## The Complete Pre-Submission Quality Checklist

### Section 1: Account Data Verification

- [ ] **Account number verified against custodian records.** The receiving custodian's account number format varies — ensure the number matches exactly. Transposed digits and wrong number of characters are the most common error in this category.
- [ ] **Account title matches exactly.** "John A. Smith" and "John Smith" are different titles. Trust accounts require the full legal name of the trust, including the date. Joint accounts require both owners listed in the correct order.
- [ ] **Account type correctly identified.** IRA, Roth IRA, SEP IRA, joint tenancy, individual brokerage, trust — each has a distinct form set. A form submitted for the wrong account type is always rejected.
- [ ] **Tax ID / SSN verified.** Nine digits, no dashes unless the custodian's form requires them. For trust accounts, verify the trust's EIN rather than the grantor's SSN.
- [ ] **Date of birth verified.** Required for IRAs and many retirement accounts. Verify against the client's original documentation, not from memory.

### Section 2: Form Version and Completeness

- [ ] **Form version is current.** [Custodians](/who-we-serve/custodians) update their forms periodically. A form from 6 months ago may have been superseded. Verify the current version number against the custodian's published form library before submission.
- [ ] **All required fields completed.** Every blank field on a custodian form is a potential NIGO trigger. Go through every field — not just the ones that were populated. Required fields left blank because they "don't apply" need a notation to that effect, not an empty box.
- [ ] **No fields completed that should be blank.** This sounds counterintuitive, but some custodian forms have fields that trigger processing flags if completed in certain circumstances (notably, fields related to IRA contribution types on transfer forms). Know the difference between required and prohibited fields for each form type.
- [ ] **Handwritten corrections absent.** Any correction on a form — even a small one — typically requires a new form with the corrected information. Crossed-out fields, white-out, and handwritten corrections are NIGO triggers on virtually every custodian's forms.

### Section 3: Signature Requirements

- [ ] **All required signatures present.** Every custodian form specifies who must sign. Individual accounts require the account owner. Joint accounts require both owners in most cases. Trust accounts require the trustee(s) — exactly as named in the trust document.
- [ ] **Signatures are in the correct locations.** Signing in the wrong signature line on a form (e.g., signing on the witness line rather than the owner line) triggers a NIGO. Verify signature placement against the form instructions.
- [ ] **Date is present with each signature.** Missing date adjacent to a signature is a NIGO trigger on most custodian forms. The date must be the actual signing date — not backdated.
- [ ] **Medallion signature guarantee present where required.** Certain transfer types — particularly transfers above threshold amounts or involving liquidation — require a Medallion Signature Guarantee. Verify requirements before submission, not after rejection.
- [ ] **Notarization present where required.** Some account types and transfer situations require notarized signatures. Verify custodian requirements by account type.

### Section 4: Transfer-Specific Requirements

- [ ] **Transfer type correctly designated.** ACATS, in-kind, partial transfer, full transfer — the designation on the form must match the intent of the transfer. A full transfer form submitted when a partial transfer is intended (or vice versa) creates processing problems that go beyond a simple NIGO.
- [ ] **Assets to be transferred correctly specified.** For partial transfers: the specific securities or accounts being transferred must be listed exactly. For in-kind transfers: the assets must be held at the delivering custodian in the form specified. Verify before submission.
- [ ] **Receiving account information complete.** The receiving custodian's account number, branch/office code where required, and routing information must be accurate. This is the mirror check to the delivering custodian's information.
- [ ] **Outstanding margin balances addressed.** Accounts with margin balances have additional requirements for transfer. Verify with the custodian's transfer team before submitting a margin account transfer — some custodians require margin balance resolution before accepting the transfer.
- [ ] **Restricted securities flagged.** Restricted or control securities often cannot be transferred via ACATS and require special handling. Identify these before submission and route them through the appropriate alternative process.

### Section 5: Beneficiary and Plan Designations

- [ ] **Beneficiary designations complete for retirement accounts.** IRA and retirement account transfers often require beneficiary information on the receiving account. Incomplete or missing beneficiary designations are a common NIGO source for retirement account transfers.
- [ ] **Primary and contingent beneficiaries specified where required.** Some custodians require both designations. Verify requirements by account type.
- [ ] **Beneficiary information matches the client's existing designation.** Unless the client is intentionally changing their beneficiary designation as part of the transition, the information should match what was established at the prior custodian.

### Section 6: Compliance and Documentation

- [ ] **Client signature date is within form acceptance window.** Most custodians reject forms signed more than 30–90 days before submission. Verify the custodian's acceptance window and ensure you're within it.
- [ ] **Audit trail documentation complete.** Every submission should be accompanied by a record of: who reviewed the form, when it was reviewed, and what validation steps were completed. This documentation protects the firm in regulatory review.
- [ ] **Submission logged in the tracking system.** Before the packet goes out, the submission should be logged: date, time, method, custodian confirmation number if available, expected processing timeline. This is operational discipline, not paperwork — it's the only way to manage exceptions effectively.

## Using This Checklist at Scale

For ops teams running high transition volume, the pre-submission checklist should be enforced systematically rather than applied ad hoc.

**Assign checklist completion to a specific role.** The specialist who builds the form packet should not be the same person who runs the pre-submission review. A second-pair-of-eyes review catches errors that the original specialist normalized during construction.

**Use the checklist as a signoff gate.** No packet submits without a completed checklist. The checklist is not a reference document — it's a control.

**Track checklist exceptions as process improvement data.** Every NIGO that occurs despite a completed checklist identifies a gap in the checklist. Over time, your firm's checklist should reflect your firm's specific NIGO history — not just a generic best-practice list.

**Automate the systematic checks.** Pre-submission validation technology can catch the mechanical errors (blank required fields, wrong form version, date outside acceptance window) automatically. Reserve human checklist attention for the judgment calls (beneficiary intent, restricted securities flags, margin balance resolution). This is the division of labor that gets ops teams to 2–4% NIGO rates.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the most common cause of NIGO rejections in repapering?**
Missing or mismatched signatures (including incorrect placement, undated signatures, or missing medallion guarantees where required), incorrect or outdated form versions, and account number format errors. These three categories account for the majority of preventable NIGO rejections across all major custodians.

**How long should a pre-submission quality review take?**
For a standard brokerage account packet, 20–30 minutes with a well-organized checklist. For complex account types (trusts, IRA rollovers, accounts with restricted securities), 45–60 minutes. The time investment is the most favorable ROI in all of transition operations given the 10+ days of delay each NIGO adds.

**Should the person who builds the form packet also run the quality review?**
No. Second-pair-of-eyes review is the standard in best-practice ops teams. The specialist who built the packet has normalized its contents — a fresh reviewer catches errors that the builder has stopped seeing. At high volume, this can be structured as a review rotation rather than requiring a dedicated second specialist.

**What form version errors most commonly trigger NIGO rejections?**
Using a form that has been superseded by the custodian without the ops team's knowledge. Custodians typically update forms quarterly or when regulatory changes require it. Without a version control system and regular update monitoring, it's easy to submit a form that was valid 6 months ago but has since been replaced.

**How do you verify the correct ACATS transfer type before submission?**
Confirm the account type (individual brokerage, IRA, joint, trust) and the nature of the transfer (full vs. partial, in-kind vs. liquidate and transfer). For each combination, the custodian's transfer guide specifies the required form and designation. This lookup should be systematized — not done from memory.

**What is the difference between a NIGO rejection and a custodian processing delay?**
A NIGO rejection is a formal rejection of the submission that requires correction and resubmission — restarting the processing clock entirely. A processing delay is a queue or volume issue on the custodian's end that does not require any action from the submitting firm. The distinction matters for exception management: NIGOs require immediate action; processing delays require monitoring.

**How do you track NIGO rates per custodian to identify patterns?**
Log every submission and its outcome by custodian, account type, and submission date. Track rejections with the stated NIGO reason. After 20–30 submissions per custodian, patterns emerge: specific form fields that generate disproportionate rejections, account types that require additional validation, submission timing that affects processing.
