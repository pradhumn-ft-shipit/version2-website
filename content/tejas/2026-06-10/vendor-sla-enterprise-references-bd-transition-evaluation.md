# Vendor SLA and Enterprise References: Evaluation Guide for 30-40 BD Transitions Per Year

When your firm is doing 4–8 transitions per year, vendor selection is mostly about features and price. The downside of a bad vendor pick is manageable — you'll absorb a few painful transitions and switch providers next year. When your firm is doing 30–40 transitions per year, the calculus changes entirely. Vendor failure at that volume isn't a quarter-long inconvenience. It's a structural risk to the BD's recruiting promise and its operational reputation.

That's why enterprise-scale BDs don't evaluate transition vendors the way mid-market firms do. The feature-comparison spreadsheet is a smaller part of the decision. The bigger parts are vendor SLAs that can be enforced at scale and enterprise references that demonstrate the vendor has actually operated at 30+ transitions per year — not just talked about it.

This is the framework for that enterprise-scale evaluation.

## The SLAs that actually matter at scale

Most transition technology vendors will hand you a 40-page service agreement with SLAs scattered throughout. At enterprise scale, only four SLAs matter — but they have to be specific, measurable, and have real financial consequences for breach.

**SLA 1: Platform availability.** The transition platform has to be available during the operational windows when transitions are active. The right target is 99.9% uptime during business hours (8 AM–8 PM ET Monday through Friday) with breach credits. A vendor that won't commit to 99.9% uptime during business hours is signaling either that they don't have the infrastructure or that they don't take operational reliability seriously. For enterprise BDs, anything less than 99.9% with breach credits is a non-starter.

**SLA 2: NIGO rate ceiling.** The vendor commits to a NIGO rate ceiling — typically 5% across the BD's overall transition volume on a rolling 12-month basis. If the NIGO rate exceeds the ceiling, the vendor takes financial responsibility for the rework and provides root-cause analysis. This SLA is what separates real transition platforms from form-generation tools. A vendor that won't commit to a NIGO rate ceiling is putting all the operational risk on the BD.

**SLA 3: Implementation support during the first 90 days.** Enterprise transitions typically have a non-trivial implementation period — provisioning, integration testing, custom workflow configuration, staff training. The SLA should specify named implementation resources, response time guarantees during implementation (4-hour response on critical issues, 24-hour on standard), and an implementation completion milestone with associated payment gates.

**SLA 4: Ongoing support response times.** For day-to-day operations, the SLA should specify response time targets for different incident severities. P1 (transition is blocked, active client risk): 1-hour response. P2 (non-blocking issue affecting current transition): 4-hour response. P3 (informational): 24-hour response. These response times have to be measurable and have credits or escalation paths for breach.

A vendor's response to a request for these specific SLAs is itself a signal. Vendors who push back hard on financial breach credits are signaling that they don't trust their own platform under stress. Vendors who accept these SLAs without much negotiation are usually either (a) operationally mature, or (b) underwriting risk they shouldn't be. The right answer is somewhere in the middle — informed negotiation with specific operational rationale for each clause.

## What enterprise references actually demonstrate

The other half of enterprise-scale evaluation is reference calls. At 30–40 transitions per year, you need references from comparable BDs — not from a mid-market RIA that does 4 transitions a year. The reference call is structured very differently than mid-market vendor evaluation.

**Reference structure 1: operational scale.** The reference customer should be doing at least 20 transitions per year on the vendor's platform, ideally 30+. The call should cover: average transition timeline, NIGO rates over the past 12 months, vendor responsiveness during incidents, and operational headcount required to support the volume. A reference that can't speak to these metrics either isn't actually using the platform at scale or isn't measuring properly.

**Reference structure 2: enterprise integration.** The reference customer should have integrated the vendor's platform with their broader operations stack — CRM, document management, compliance tooling, custodial relationships. The call should cover: integration quality, configuration flexibility, and how the vendor handled enterprise-grade requirements that differ from mid-market default behavior.

**Reference structure 3: incident response history.** Every vendor will have had incidents. The interesting question is how they handled them. The reference call should ask about the worst incident the customer experienced — what happened, how the vendor responded, what was the resolution time, what was the financial accountability. Vendors who haven't had any incidents at the reference customer either aren't being used at scale or are getting lucky.

**Reference structure 4: vendor stability and roadmap alignment.** Enterprise selections are 3-year decisions. The reference call should cover: how long has the customer been on the platform, what's the trajectory of the vendor relationship, what does the customer think of the vendor's roadmap, and is there any indication the customer is evaluating alternatives. A reference customer who has been on the platform 12+ months and is committed to staying is a very different signal than one who switched on 6 months ago.

A common mistake at enterprise scale is taking too few references — one or two. The right number is 4–6 references from BDs of comparable size and transition volume. Patterns across multiple references reveal much more than any single conversation.

## Procurement questions enterprise BDs should be asking

Beyond SLAs and references, there are 15 procurement questions that separate vendors who can operate at enterprise scale from vendors who only sound like they can.

**On security and compliance:**

What is your SOC 2 Type 2 status, and when was your last audit? What's your data residency model, and how does it handle client PII for multi-state transitions? How do you handle compliance evidence for FINRA and SEC examinations? What's your business continuity and disaster recovery plan, and have you tested it in the past 12 months?

**On operational scale:**

What's the largest single transition (by AUM and account count) you've successfully processed? What's the highest concurrent transition load any single customer has run on your platform? What's your platform throughput ceiling, and how does it scale with customer growth? What's the median and 95th percentile transition timeline across your customer base?

**On integration:**

Which CRMs, custodial systems, document management platforms, and compliance tools do you natively integrate with? What's the typical integration timeline for a custom enterprise integration? Do you support SSO, SCIM, and enterprise identity management standards?

**On contract and commercial terms:**

What's your [pricing](/pricing) model at the volume we're projecting (30–40 transitions/year)? What's your minimum commitment, and what's the termination flexibility? Do you offer per-transition pricing as an alternative to seat-based licensing? What's your data-portability commitment if we exit the contract?

The answers to these 15 questions, taken together, build a picture of whether the vendor is operationally ready for enterprise-scale deployment. Vendors who can answer all of them with specifics are usually mature. Vendors who deflect on more than 3–4 are usually not.

## The reference call script

The reference call is where most enterprise vendor evaluations fall short. Most procurement teams ask generic questions ("Are you happy with the vendor?") and get generic answers ("Yes, mostly"). At enterprise scale, the reference call has to extract specific operational information.

The 30-minute reference call should follow a structured script:

**Minutes 0–5: scale and context.** How many transitions per year are you running on the platform? What's your transition team headcount? What types of advisors are you typically transitioning?

**Minutes 5–15: operational performance.** What's your average transition timeline on the platform? What's your NIGO rate? How does your AUM retention compare to your historical baseline before adopting the platform?

**Minutes 15–25: incident and support history.** Tell me about the worst incident you've experienced. What broke? How did the vendor respond? What was the financial and operational impact?

**Minutes 25–30: vendor relationship.** Would you sign the same contract again? Is there anything you'd negotiate differently? Are you evaluating alternatives?

The structured script forces the conversation past generalities and into the specifics that actually inform the procurement decision. A reference customer who can speak fluently to all four sections is providing high-signal information. A customer who deflects on incidents or roadmap is providing a different kind of signal — typically that the relationship is more political than operational.

## What the SLA and reference framework reveals about vendor readiness

The combination of SLA negotiation and structured reference calls reveals four enterprise-readiness signals that the standard feature comparison cannot.

**Signal 1: operational maturity.** Vendors with mature operations can answer specific operational questions, commit to enterprise SLAs, and produce references who speak credibly to enterprise-scale deployment. Vendors without that maturity either deflect or commit to terms they can't actually meet.

**Signal 2: financial alignment.** Vendors who accept financial breach credits in SLAs are signaling confidence in their platform. Vendors who negotiate hard against any financial accountability are signaling either platform fragility or aggressive commercial posture — both worth diligencing further.

**Signal 3: customer success orientation.** Reference customers who feel heard by the vendor — whose feedback has shaped product roadmap, whose operational requirements have driven integration improvements — are signaling a vendor that treats enterprise customers as partners rather than as account farms.

**Signal 4: incident learning.** Vendors who use incidents to drive improvements (rather than treating each one as a one-time event) build platforms that get better under stress. The reference customer's incident story is one of the highest-signal pieces of information in the evaluation.

A BD doing 30–40 transitions per year cannot afford to get this wrong. The cost of a bad vendor selection at that scale is measured in months of recovery, lost recruits who hear about the operational pain, and reputational damage that takes years to undo. The SLA and reference framework is what separates the enterprise-grade selections from the marketing-driven ones.

For BD operations teams running this evaluation for the first time, the most important advice is: take longer than feels necessary. A 6-month enterprise evaluation that surfaces the right vendor is dramatically cheaper than a 6-week evaluation that surfaces the wrong one.

## Frequently asked questions

**What uptime SLA should an enterprise BD require for a transition platform?**

99.9% uptime during business hours (8 AM-8 PM ET, Monday-Friday) with financial breach credits is the enterprise standard. Some vendors offer 99.95%, but the marginal benefit at that level is small. The more important question is whether the SLA includes financial breach credits — without them, the SLA is largely informational rather than operational.

**Is a NIGO-rate SLA reasonable to negotiate from a transition technology vendor?**

Yes, at enterprise volume (20+ transitions per year), a NIGO-rate ceiling SLA is reasonable and increasingly common. The typical target is 5% NIGO rate on a rolling 12-month basis, with vendor financial responsibility for rework above the ceiling. Vendors who refuse this SLA are typically signaling that they don't have confidence in their form generation under stress.

**How many enterprise references should an evaluation require?**

For a 30-40 transition-per-year BD, the right number is 4-6 enterprise references from comparably sized firms. Patterns across multiple references reveal far more than any single conversation. A single positive reference is often political; consistent themes across 4-6 references are diagnostic.

**What's the most important question to ask in an enterprise reference call?**

The incident story. Every vendor has had incidents — what matters is how they handled them. A 5-10 minute conversation about the worst incident the reference customer experienced reveals more about vendor maturity than any other single question. Vendors who handle incidents with financial accountability and process improvement are operationally mature; vendors who don't, aren't.

**Should an enterprise BD negotiate per-transition pricing or seat-based licensing?**

For 30-40 transitions per year, per-transition pricing typically produces better economics if available. Seat-based licensing makes sense if the BD has consistent year-over-year volume and wants budgetary predictability. The negotiation should clarify the breakpoints where each pricing model becomes favorable and structure the contract for the actual expected volume profile.

**What's the typical implementation timeline for an enterprise transition platform?**

For a 30-40 transition-per-year BD with integrations to CRM, document management, and custodial systems, expect 60-90 days of implementation work before the first production transition. Vendors who promise 30-day enterprise implementations are typically underestimating integration complexity or pushing the implementation risk onto the BD's internal team.
