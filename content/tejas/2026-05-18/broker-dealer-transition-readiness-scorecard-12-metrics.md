There are twelve operational metrics that predict AUM leakage in broker-dealer transitions. Most BDs track fewer than four of them — and the ones they skip are usually the most predictive.

AUM leakage during advisor transitions is not random. It follows a pattern: when transitions take longer, clients have more time to reconsider. When forms get rejected, advisors get frustrated and clients get nervous. When operations teams lose visibility, problems compound silently until accounts are already gone. The $19B in annual asset losses from advisor transitions is not a market force — it's an operational failure that shows up in specific, measurable places.

The firms that hold onto the most assets during transition periods are the ones running against a defined set of metrics. Here is the scorecard.

## Why AUM Leakage Is Predictable

Eighteen thousand advisors switch firms annually. When an advisor moves a $500M book of business at 0.8% annual fees, each day of transition represents roughly $10,000 in revenue risk. The longer the transfer window stays open, the longer the departing firm has an opportunity to retain those clients, and the longer clients have to question whether following their advisor is the right move.

Wirehouse market share has been declining — from 31.4% in 2022 toward a projected 29.3% by 2026 — in part because the firms advisors move to can execute transitions faster. Speed of transfer is now a competitive variable, not a fixed operational constraint.

The twelve metrics below don't just describe how well your transitions are going. They tell you, before a transition starts, whether your operations are positioned to retain assets or to lose them.

## The 12-Metric Scorecard

Score each metric from 1 (critical risk) to 5 (best-in-class). Total score guide: 12–40 = critical risk, 41–70 = moderate, 71–100+ = best-in-class.

**Metric 1 — NIGO Rate**
*What it measures:* Percentage of submitted forms rejected by [custodians](/who-we-serve/custodians) as Not In Good Order.
*Red flag threshold:* Above 15%
*Why it matters:* Every NIGO resets the clock on that account. A 20% NIGO rate on a 500-account book means 100 accounts in limbo, each requiring new client signatures and resubmission. Manual operations typically see NIGO rates of 15–40%; AI-powered pre-submission validation reduces this below 5%.

**Metric 2 — Average Days to Account Live**
*What it measures:* Time from paperwork submission to account active at receiving custodian.
*Red flag threshold:* Above 45 days
*Why it matters:* This is the core vulnerability window. Industry average without automation is 90 days. Firms running purpose-built transition platforms average 18–25 days. Every day beyond 21 is a day of unnecessary exposure.

**Metric 3 — Custodian Coverage**
*What it measures:* Number of custodians for which your operations team has automated form workflows.
*Red flag threshold:* Fewer than 3 custodians with full automation
*Why it matters:* Advisors increasingly hold client assets across multiple custodians. If your team can automate Schwab but handles [Fidelity](https://www.fidelity.com/wealth-services/working-with-fidelity) and [Pershing](https://www.pershing.com) manually, you're creating two-speed transitions that extend timelines for the manually-handled accounts.

**Metric 4 — Ops FTE per Transition**
*What it measures:* Number of dedicated staff hours (in FTE terms) required per advisor transition.
*Red flag threshold:* Above 1.0 FTE per transition
*Why it matters:* Manual transitions are staff-intensive and create a capacity ceiling. When your ops team is stretched across 20 simultaneous transitions, quality drops. Operations burnout and turnover is a real cost — replacing an ops specialist runs $30,000–$60,000.

**Metric 5 — AUM Retention Rate**
*What it measures:* Percentage of the transitioning advisor's AUM that successfully follows to your firm.
*Red flag threshold:* Below 85%
*Why it matters:* Advisors who move firms successfully transfer 85%+ of client assets on average (3XEquity research). Firms landing below this threshold have an execution problem, not just a relationship problem.

**Metric 6 — Client Communication Score**
*What it measures:* Percentage of clients proactively updated at each milestone of the transition.
*Red flag threshold:* Below 70%
*Why it matters:* Client confusion about where assets "live" during transition is a top driver of decisions not to follow an advisor. Automated milestone notifications — form submitted, transfer initiated, account live — reduce anxiety and reduce attrition.

**Metric 7 — Form Error Rate**
*What it measures:* Percentage of forms with data entry errors before pre-submission review.
*Red flag threshold:* Above 10%
*Why it matters:* Form errors are a leading indicator of future NIGOs. Tracking them before submission (not just after rejection) tells you whether your data collection and form population process is working or whether you're relying on custodian rejections to catch errors.

**Metric 8 — SLA Compliance**
*What it measures:* Percentage of transitions completed within the timeline you committed to the advisor.
*Red flag threshold:* Below 80%
*Why it matters:* Advisors who were promised a 30-day transition and got a 75-day transition do not refer other advisors to your firm. Your SLA compliance rate is your recruiting reputation metric — it just doesn't show up in any deck until it's already costing you pipeline.

**Metric 9 — Compliance Audit Readiness**
*What it measures:* Percentage of completed transitions with a full, exportable audit trail.
*Red flag threshold:* Below 95%
*Why it matters:* FINRA's 2026 Regulatory Oversight Report flagged recordkeeping deficiencies over 50 times. A transition without a complete audit trail is an exam finding waiting to happen. This metric should be close to 100% — anything below 95% indicates process gaps.

**Metric 10 — Advisor Satisfaction Score**
*What it measures:* Post-transition NPS from advisors who completed a transition through your firm.
*Red flag threshold:* Below 7/10
*Why it matters:* Advisor satisfaction directly predicts referral behavior. Advisors who had a clean, fast transition tell their network. Advisors who spent 90 days chasing forms and status updates do not. This is the only metric on this list that measures the recruiting downstream effect of transition quality.

**Metric 11 — Recruiting Pipeline Velocity**
*What it measures:* Days from signed offer to advisor in production.
*Red flag threshold:* Above 90 days
*Why it matters:* This is the composite output metric. It includes regulatory approval time, technology onboarding, and the transition itself. If your transition operations take 90 days alone, you're starting 90 days behind before counting anything else.

**Metric 12 — Technology Stack Coverage**
*What it measures:* Percentage of core transition workflow steps that are automated rather than manual.
*Red flag threshold:* Below 50% automated
*Why it matters:* This is the structural metric. It tells you how much of your risk exposure is systemic. A firm where less than half the workflow is automated is not just slower — it's exposed to human error at every manual touchpoint.

## How to Score Your BD

Add up your scores across all twelve metrics (1–5 each, maximum 60):

- **12–25:** Critical risk. Three or more metrics at red-flag threshold. Immediate remediation required before scaling recruiting.
- **26–40:** High risk. Operational gaps are limiting both AUM retention and recruiting capacity. Prioritize Metrics 1, 2, and 9 first.
- **41–50:** Moderate. Your fundamentals are in place, but execution consistency is inconsistent. Focus on SLA compliance and advisor satisfaction scores.
- **51–60:** Best-in-class. Continue tracking. The firms that fall from this tier do so when they scale rapidly without scaling operations first.

## The Three Most Predictive Metrics

If you only have time to focus on three, the research is clear on which ones drive the most AUM retention impact:

**NIGO Rate (Metric 1):** The single most direct lever on transition time. Reducing NIGOs from 20% to 2% on a 500-account book removes months from the average timeline.

**Days to Account Live (Metric 2):** The headline metric. This is what advisors will tell their peers. Getting this below 30 days is achievable with purpose-built automation; it requires software infrastructure, not just better processes.

**Compliance Audit Readiness (Metric 9):** The risk metric that most BDs underestimate until they're in an exam. Exam findings from transitions are expensive — in time, in attention, and occasionally in fines. Full audit trail coverage is non-negotiable.

## What Best-in-Class BDs Do Differently

Firms with best-in-class scores share three operational characteristics. First, they use pre-submission validation that checks forms against each custodian's specific current requirements — not a generic quality review, but a rule-based check against Fidelity's requirements, Schwab's requirements, and Pershing's requirements separately.

Second, they have real-time status visibility for every active transition. Their ops team is not making status calls to advisors — they have a dashboard that shows every transition by stage, flags at-risk accounts, and triggers proactive client communication automatically.

Third, they've decoupled operations capacity from headcount. Manual operations hit a ceiling around 15 simultaneous transitions; best-in-class BDs can handle 40+ with the same team because their workflows are built on software, not spreadsheets.

**Frequently Asked Questions**

**What is AUM leakage and how does it occur during advisor transitions?**
AUM leakage is the loss of client assets that should have transferred to the receiving firm but didn't — because clients chose not to follow, delayed signing paperwork, or moved assets elsewhere during the transition window. Leakage is directly correlated with transition length: the longer the window, the more opportunities for assets to leave.

**What metrics most predict AUM loss during broker-dealer transitions?**
NIGO rate, average days to account live, and ops FTE per transition are the three most predictive. A high NIGO rate extends the timeline; extended timelines increase client attrition; high ops FTE per transition limits your capacity to scale recruiting.

**What is an acceptable NIGO rejection rate for broker-dealer transitions?**
Below 5% is achievable with pre-submission validation technology. Manual operations typically see 15–40%. Above 10% is a red flag requiring process review; above 20% indicates systemic form population or validation gaps.

**How does transition time correlate with AUM retention?**
Directly. Each day the transition window remains open is another day a client can reconsider following their advisor. Firms completing transitions in under 30 days consistently outperform 90-day operations on AUM retention.

**What are the most common operational failure points in BD advisor transitions?**
NIGO accumulation from missing pre-submission validation, manual status tracking that loses visibility above 10–15 concurrent transitions, and inconsistent client communication that creates confusion and anxiety during the transfer window.

**How do leading broker-dealers benchmark their transition operations?**
The firms benchmarking most rigorously track all twelve metrics on this scorecard and review them quarterly. Most BDs start with NIGO rate and days to account live, then layer in the structural metrics (technology stack coverage, ops FTE per transition) once they have the baseline.
