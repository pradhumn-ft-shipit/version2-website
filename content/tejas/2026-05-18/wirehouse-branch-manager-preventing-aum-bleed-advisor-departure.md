A $500M book. Ninety days of transition. At 0.8% annual fees, each day of that window represents roughly $10,000 in revenue risk — not theoretical risk, but the kind that walks out the door when a client decides to follow their advisor to a new firm rather than wait for paperwork to clear.

Wirehouse market share has been declining steadily — from 31.4% in 2022 toward a projected 29.3% by 2026. The net outflow of advisors continues: wirehouses lost a net 612 advisors in just nine months of 2023. Branch managers managing departures understand the pattern. What they often don't have is a systematic framework for closing the one variable they can control: the length of the transition window.

Non-solicitation agreements limit what departing advisors can say. They don't limit what clients can decide.

## Why Advisor Departures Create an AUM Bleed Window

When an advisor leaves, there is a period — sometimes days, sometimes weeks, sometimes months — between their departure and the completion of client account transfers. During this period, clients are in an ambiguous state. Their assets are technically still at the wirehouse, but their relationship is with an advisor who is no longer there.

This ambiguity is not neutral. Clients who haven't heard from the new advisor, who don't understand what's happening to their accounts, or who are receiving calls from their former advisor's assistant asking about their plans — these clients are actively at risk.

The "transition window" is the period between advisor departure and completed transfer. Making that window as short as possible is the single highest-leverage action available to the receiving firm's operations team. It is also, notably, a variable controlled entirely by operations, not by relationship management.

## The 5 Root Causes of AUM Bleed During Advisor Transitions

**1. NIGO rejections that restart the clock**
Not In Good Order rejections are the most direct cause of extended transition windows. When a form is rejected by a custodian — for a missing field, an incorrect account number format, an outdated form version — the affected account requires new client signatures and a full resubmission. Each NIGO adds days or weeks to that account's timeline. Manual operations see NIGO rates of 15–40%; without pre-submission validation, each rejection is a call to a client who is already anxious about their accounts.

**2. Delayed form delivery to clients**
The transition window opens the day the advisor leaves. If it takes a week or two to deliver paperwork to clients — still common in operations that rely on paper forms or unstructured email processes — the window extends before any actual custodian processing has even begun. Every day between advisor departure and client paperwork delivery is pure exposure.

**3. No real-time visibility for operations teams**
When operations teams can't see which clients have signed paperwork and which haven't, follow-up is reactive rather than proactive. The accounts that haven't been touched in two weeks don't surface until they become problem accounts. Real-time status visibility allows proactive outreach to clients before inaction turns into attrition.

**4. Multi-custodian complexity slows processing**
Advisors with books spread across multiple [custodians](/who-we-serve/custodians) create parallel workflows that operations teams must manage simultaneously. Without systematic multi-custodian support, each custodian relationship requires its own process, and the slowest custodian determines the overall timeline. An advisor with Schwab, [Fidelity](https://www.fidelity.com/wealth-services/working-with-fidelity), and [Pershing](https://www.pershing.com) accounts is three separate workflows in a manual environment.

**5. Manual status tracking creates follow-up gaps**
Spreadsheet-based status tracking fails above about 15 simultaneous account moves. When an operations specialist is managing hundreds of accounts across multiple custodians, things fall through. The accounts that don't surface as active problems stop receiving attention — until they become attrition.

## What Good Looks Like: 3 Weeks vs. 90 Days

The industry average for advisor transitions without automation is approximately 90 days. Purpose-built [transition automation](/solutions/advisor-transitions) reduces this to 18–25 days. The mechanism is not faster custodian processing — custodians operate on fixed timelines. The mechanism is eliminating the delay that accumulates before and between custodian interactions: NIGO-caused restarts, form delivery delays, manual status gaps, and multi-custodian coordination friction.

For a $500M AUM transition at 0.8% annual fees:
- 90-day transition: 90 days of revenue at risk
- 24-day transition: 24 days of revenue at risk
- **Revenue captured by closing the window 66 days earlier: approximately $660,000**

That is not the revenue the receiving firm captures by winning the advisor. That is the additional revenue the receiving firm captures simply by being operationally faster than the industry average. The advisor's clients were already going to follow — the question is whether any of them got impatient, confused, or solicited during the window.

A 95% reduction in NIGOs — achievable with custodian-specific pre-submission validation — eliminates the single largest source of timeline extension. A real-time status dashboard eliminates the follow-up gap. Together, these two operational changes are the primary drivers of the 75% faster transition benchmark.

## The Operations Stack That Minimizes Transition Windows

The four capabilities that directly reduce AUM bleed exposure are:

**Pre-submission form validation against each custodian's specific requirements.** Not generic form review — validation against the current field requirements, acceptable formats, and required documentation for Fidelity specifically, Schwab specifically, Pershing specifically. Generic review catches obvious errors; custodian-specific validation catches the errors that generate NIGOs.

**Single-entry data population across all forms.** Client data entered once, populated across all required forms for all custodians. Eliminates manual re-entry errors (the leading cause of NIGO rejections in manual environments) and eliminates the time cost of re-typing the same information into six different forms.

**Centralized status dashboard with proactive alerts.** Real-time visibility into which clients have signed, which are pending, and which are approaching risk thresholds. Automated follow-up triggers for accounts that haven't moved in a defined number of days. This converts reactive status management into a systematic process.

**Automated client communication at each milestone.** Notifications to clients when forms are delivered, when transfers are submitted, and when accounts are confirmed live. Clients who receive proactive status updates are less anxious, less likely to call their departing advisor for information, and less likely to make decisions based on confusion about what's happening.

## What Branch Managers Should Demand from Transition Technology

If your firm is evaluating transition technology — or currently relying on manual processes and accepting the AUM exposure that comes with it — these are the questions worth asking:

**Can they demonstrate real-time NIGO prevention?** Ask for a demo of what happens when a form would fail custodian validation. Can the system catch it before submission? What does the resolution workflow look like? A platform that documents NIGOs after they happen is not the same as a platform that prevents them.

**Does it support your specific custodian relationships?** Multi-custodian support is listed on most vendor websites. What matters is whether the platform has current, specific validation rules for each custodian — not just the ability to submit to multiple custodians manually.

**How does it integrate with your CRM?** For large BDs, standalone transition technology that doesn't connect to the existing client data environment creates its own data re-entry problem. Integration with Salesforce, Redtail, or Wealthbox is the difference between a workflow tool and a systems addition.

**What does the audit trail look like?** FINRA's 2026 Regulatory Oversight Report flagged recordkeeping deficiencies over 50 times. Every transition should produce a complete, exportable audit trail — who submitted what, when, what changes were made, how rejections were resolved. This protects the firm operationally and during regulatory examinations.

## The Calculation Branch Managers Should Run

Take your firm's average advisor book size. Multiply by your fee rate. Divide by 365 to get daily revenue. Multiply by your average transition time in days. That's your current transition window revenue exposure per advisor transition.

Now calculate what 75% faster would look like: the same advisor book, 22 days instead of 90. That's the revenue that stays captured rather than at risk.

For a firm doing 20 advisor transitions per year with an average book of $200M at 0.8% fees, the difference between 90-day transitions and 24-day transitions is approximately $5 million per year in reduced revenue exposure. Not revenue lost and recovered — revenue that simply never entered the at-risk window.

That's the AUM bleed conversation worth having with your operations team.

**Frequently Asked Questions**

**How do wirehouses prevent AUM loss when advisors leave?**
By minimizing the transition window — the period between advisor departure and completed client account transfers. The shorter this window, the less time clients have to reconsider following their advisor or to be solicited by the departing firm. This requires pre-submission form validation, automated client communication, and real-time status visibility.

**What triggers AUM bleed during advisor transitions?**
The primary triggers are: NIGO rejections that extend timelines, delayed form delivery to clients, lack of real-time status visibility, multi-custodian complexity, and reactive (rather than proactive) client communication during the transfer window.

**How long is the average advisor transition?**
Without automation, industry average is approximately 90 days. Purpose-built transition automation reduces this to 18–25 days. The difference is almost entirely operational — custodians process transfers on similar timelines; the gains come from eliminating NIGO-caused delays, manual re-entry errors, and status management gaps.

**What is the revenue impact of a faster advisor transition?**
For a $500M AUM book at 0.8% annual fees, each day of transition represents approximately $10,000 in revenue at risk. Reducing transition time from 90 days to 24 days eliminates 66 days of exposure — approximately $660,000 in additional revenue captured per transition.

**What technology prevents NIGO rejections during advisor transitions?**
Pre-submission validation technology that checks forms against each custodian's specific current requirements before submission. This is different from general form review — it validates against Fidelity's rules, Schwab's rules, and Pershing's rules separately, catching custodian-specific errors before they generate rejections.

**How does client communication reduce AUM bleed?**
Automated milestone notifications — forms delivered, transfer submitted, account live — keep clients informed without requiring advisor intervention. Clients who receive proactive updates are less anxious and less likely to make decisions based on confusion or silence during the transfer window.
