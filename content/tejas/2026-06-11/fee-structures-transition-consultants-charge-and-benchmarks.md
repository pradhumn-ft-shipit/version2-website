# What Transition Consultants Actually Charge: A Fee Structure Benchmark for 2026

The transition consulting market is one of the most economically opaque corners of wealth management. Two advisors leaving the same wirehouse with similar book sizes can pay fees that differ by a factor of ten — and neither can easily tell whether they got a good deal. [Pricing](/pricing) is rarely posted publicly. Proposals come in five different formats. The fee structure shapes the consultant's behavior far more than the dollar amount does.

This is a benchmark of what transition consultants actually charge in 2026 across the five pricing models: flat fee, per-account, monthly retainer, success-based, and AUM percentage. For each we cover the real dollar range, what it covers, what behavior it incentivizes, and where it breaks. Then we cover the change nobody in consulting wants to discuss publicly: how AI automation is reshaping unit economics, letting consultants who adopt it charge less and earn more.

This isn't a takedown. The good consultants earn every dollar. It's a buyer's guide for advisors about to sign a six-figure engagement.

## The five fee structures that dominate the market

Every transition consultant proposal we've seen in the last 24 months fits into one of five structures, sometimes blended. Each creates different incentives and fits different transition profiles.

| Fee structure | Typical 2026 range | What it covers | Best fit | What it incentivizes |
|---------------|--------------------|----|----------|----------------------|
| Flat fee | $25K–$150K per transition | Full repapering and project management for one advisor or small team | Single-advisor breakaways with predictable scope | Scope discipline; speed; clean handoff |
| Per-account | $75–$200 per account | Account-by-account repapering, NIGO resolution | Mid-sized books (200–800 accounts), variable account complexity | Account volume; thoroughness on each account |
| Monthly retainer | $8K–$25K per month | Ongoing transition support, multiple concurrent advisors | Recruiting firms and OSJs with continuous transition pipeline | Stability of consultant revenue; depth of relationship |
| Success-based | 1–3% of transferred AUM | End-to-end transition tied to AUM retention | High-stakes transitions ($100M+ books), risk-tolerant firms | AUM retention; client outreach quality |
| AUM percentage | 0.10–0.25% of transferred AUM (one-time) | Comprehensive transition project | Mega RIAs onboarding $250M+ books | Total AUM moved; minimization of attrition |

The benchmarks above are mid-market 2026 figures for U.S. transitions. The high end of every range applies to wirehouse-to-RIA transitions with complex trust, alternative, or institutional accounts. The low end applies to RIA-to-RIA moves with standard custodial relationships at Schwab, [Fidelity](https://www.fidelity.com/wealth-services/working-with-fidelity), or Altruist.

## Flat fee: $25,000 to $150,000 per transition

The flat fee model is the most common for single-advisor breakaways. The consultant prices the entire transition as one project — defined scope, defined timeline, single number. A $25K engagement typically covers a smaller book (under 100 accounts) with clean documentation and a straightforward custodial move. A $150K engagement covers a large wirehouse team with 500+ accounts, multiple trust relationships, alternative investments, and a complex multi-custodian setup.

What's actually included varies more than the dollar figure suggests. Most flat-fee engagements include client account inventory, ACATS submission and tracking, repapering for new custodial agreements, NIGO resolution, and project management. Often excluded: client outreach calls, portfolio management decisions, tax-loss harvesting analysis, and post-transition relationship work beyond 30 days.

What flat fee incentivizes is speed and scope discipline. The consultant has every reason to finish quickly because every additional week eats into margin. The risk: a flat-fee consultant under margin pressure can cut corners on harder accounts — trust accounts, alternatives, in-kind transfers that take three rounds of paperwork. Smart advisors confirm exception accounts are in scope, not "additional services."

Where flat fee breaks: transitions with unpredictable scope. If you don't know whether 300 or 600 accounts will move, a flat fee will either be priced for the worst case (you overpay) or the average case (the consultant loses money and quality suffers).

## Per-account pricing: $75 to $200 per account

Per-account pricing is the cleanest unit-economic model in the market. The consultant charges a fixed amount per account that moves — typically $75 to $200, with $100–$125 being the most common 2026 midpoint. A $300M book with 350 accounts at $125 per account comes to $43,750, in line with a comparable flat-fee engagement.

The structure shines when account counts are uncertain and work per account is reasonably uniform. It tracks the consultant's actual workload, so neither party argues about scope changes mid-transition. It's common at consulting firms with repeatable account-level workflows.

What it incentivizes is volume. The consultant earns more by moving more accounts, which usually aligns with the advisor's goal of retaining clients. But the model can incentivize moving accounts that arguably shouldn't — small dormant accounts more economically retired than transferred.

The premium tier ($150–$200) includes higher-touch services: thorough document review, client outreach support, white-glove NIGO resolution. The budget tier ($75–$100) is purely transactional — repapering only, advisor handles client communication separately.

## Monthly retainer: $8,000 to $25,000 per month

The retainer model is built for firms running a continuous transition pipeline — [RIA aggregators](/who-we-serve/acquisitive-rias), OSJs, recruiting-heavy broker-dealers, and consulting partnerships that support a roster of advisors over time. Instead of paying per transition, the firm pays a monthly fee and the consultant handles whatever volume comes through.

Typical 2026 ranges: $8K per month for a part-time consultant handling 2–4 transitions per year. $15K per month for a full-time embedded consultant handling 8–12 transitions annually. $25K+ per month for a senior consultant or small team supporting 20+ transitions per year.

Retainers incentivize a stable consultant-client relationship. The consultant invests in learning the firm's systems, [custodians](/who-we-serve/custodians), and preferred process — knowledge that compounds over many transitions. The flip side: when volume is low, the firm pays for unused capacity. When volume spikes, quality drops without clear contractual remedy.

The retainer model creates a quiet incentive misalignment: the consultant has no economic urgency to finish any specific transition fast. At $500M AUM and 0.8% fees, every saved day is roughly $10K in additional captured revenue. Retainer engagements need explicit SLAs on transition timelines or slow drift sets in.

## Success-based pricing: 1% to 3% of transferred AUM

Success-based pricing is the most aggressive structure in the market. The consultant takes 1% to 3% of the AUM that actually transfers, paid as a one-time fee after completion. A $200M transition at 2% is $4 million.

Success-based engagements are rare but increasingly common in two contexts: very large breakaway teams ($500M+) where the firm wants to align consultant economics with retention, and high-risk transitions a flat-fee consultant wouldn't take at any reasonable price. The model is also used by consultants who simultaneously serve as recruiters or capital partners.

The economics for the consultant can be excellent when retention is high. A 2% success fee on a $300M transition with 95% retention is $5.7 million. That shifts incentives toward end-to-end retention rather than just paperwork completion. Good success-fee consultants spend disproportionate time on client outreach quality, sentiment tracking, and aggressive [dark-period compression after wirehouse resignation](https://fasttrackr.ai/blog/dark-period-wirehouse-resignation-tools), because those activities move retention by percentage points.

Where success-based breaks: smaller transitions. A 2% success fee on a $50M transition is $1 million, wildly out of proportion to the work. The model also creates measurement friction — what counts as transferred AUM, what counts as "retained" at 90 days versus 12 months. These details belong in the contract, not a side conversation.

## AUM percentage as a flat charge: 0.10% to 0.25%

Distinct from success-based pricing, some consultants quote a one-time AUM percentage as a flat charge regardless of retention outcome. This is most common with mega RIA platforms onboarding advisors as part of their growth strategy. The 2026 range is 0.10% to 0.25% of book AUM, charged once at engagement close.

The math: a 0.15% one-time charge on a $300M transition is $450,000 — roughly a senior flat-fee engagement plus a margin for integrated platform value. Mega RIAs use this structure because it's simple to communicate ("you'll pay us 15 basis points of your book one time") and scales cleanly.

For independent advisors comparing options, the AUM-percentage flat charge looks expensive at first glance — but it usually bundles platform integration, technology onboarding, and ongoing operations support that a standalone consultant wouldn't provide. The right comparison is to the all-in cost of going independent, not to a flat-fee transition project alone.

What it incentivizes is total AUM growth. The firm wants the largest possible book onto the platform and retained long-term, because both the one-time fee and ongoing platform revenue scale with AUM.

## How the math changes when AI automation is in the stack

The last 18 months have rewritten the cost structure [for transition consultants](/who-we-serve/transition-consultants) who have adopted AI-driven automation. Proposals from those consultants look meaningfully different from proposals from consultants who haven't.

Historically, the labor cost of a transition was the cost of operations specialists doing repetitive document work — pulling account data, filling new-custodian forms, chasing signatures, resolving NIGOs, tracking ACATS. A senior ops specialist costs $30K–$60K to replace per departure and burns out predictably. A flat-fee consultant pricing a $75K engagement is largely pricing three to six weeks of that specialist work, plus overhead and margin.

AI automation collapses that labor cost. A transition platform that automates form generation, NIGO prevention, ACATS submission, and status tracking can reduce manual repapering by roughly 90%. The same engagement that required 250 hours of specialist time now requires 25–40 hours. The consultant's cost base on a $75K engagement drops by tens of thousands.

This creates two strategic positions. Position one: keep prices steady and expand margins, banking productivity as profit. Position two: drop prices to win volume, doing more transitions at lower per-engagement fees while earning more in absolute dollars. Both are happening in parallel.

Concrete economics for position two: a consultant who could previously do 24 flat-fee transitions at $75K each ($1.8M revenue, 35% margin = $630K) can now do 50 transitions at $50K each with AI in the stack ($2.5M revenue, 50% margin = $1.25M). The consultant nearly doubles earnings, charges less, and ships more advisors. Advisors get a better price and faster turnaround.

For per-account pricing, AI compresses unit cost so aggressively that the floor of the $75–$200 range is moving. Leading firms quote $50–$60 per account on standardized custodial transfers because AI tooling makes the work nearly automated. The higher end still applies for exception-heavy accounts.

For retainer engagements, AI lets a single consultant credibly support 50% to 100% more concurrent transitions than pre-AI. Firms running continuous programs are renegotiating retainer scope: same fee, double the throughput, or same throughput at reduced cost.

For success-based pricing, AI changes the risk profile. The 1–3% range still applies, but consultants with AI in their stack can take success engagements they previously would have declined because the cost of being wrong is lower.

## How to actually evaluate a proposal

If you're evaluating a transition consultant proposal in 2026, the structure matters more than the dollar figure. Here's what to ask.

Start with scope. What's included and what's "additional services"? Most disputes start here. Get a written list of exception cases — trust accounts, alternatives, in-kind transfers — and confirm they're in scope at the quoted price.

Ask about the consultant's AI stack. Consultants without modern tooling are running a cost structure 12 to 24 months out of date. That structure shows up as higher prices, longer timelines, more NIGOs. If the consultant can't articulate what they automate, you're paying for someone else's spreadsheet.

Get specific on timeline. Wirehouse-to-RIA transitions that used to take 90 days now run in three weeks at well-tooled consultants. If the proposal quotes 60 days, the consultant is not at the frontier.

Ask about NIGO rates. Modern transition platforms achieve a 95% reduction in NIGOs against legacy processes. A consultant whose NIGO rate is "we resolve them as they come up" is committing you to weeks of avoidable rework.

Finally, ask what the consultant does if a client gets pulled by the old firm's retention team. The answer separates real operational expertise from project management. A consultant with a structured response — sentiment tracking, escalation playbooks, same-day touch protocol — operates at a different level than one who shrugs and says "we move fast."

## What we recommend

For single-advisor breakaways with a clear book of $50M to $300M, flat fee or per-account pricing is usually the cleanest structure. Negotiate scope tightly, confirm AI tooling, lock in a timeline commitment.

For firms running a continuous transition pipeline, retainer engagements with explicit per-transition SLAs make sense — but only if the consultant uses modern tooling. Otherwise you're paying retainer for capacity AI-equipped competitors deliver for less. Our [transition consultant playbook for 50 annual transitions](https://fasttrackr.ai/blog/transition-consultant-playbook-50-annual-transitions) covers what high-volume operations actually look like.

For very large breakaways ($300M+) where retention is the dominant economic factor, success-based pricing aligns incentives well — but the contract must define retention measurement clearly.

For mega RIA platforms onboarding advisors as part of a growth program, the one-time AUM-percentage charge is reasonable if it bundles meaningful platform integration value.

In every case, the right question is not "what's the price?" but "what's the price for the work I'm actually getting, compared to a consultant who has modernized their stack?" Transitions don't have to be this hard. The market is correcting toward lower prices and faster timelines, and the consultants leading that correction are the ones to engage.
