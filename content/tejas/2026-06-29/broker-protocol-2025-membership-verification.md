Before you resign to go independent, confirm in writing that both the firm you are leaving and the entity you are joining are current Broker Protocol members on your resignation date. Membership is not permanent. Firms join and withdraw over time, and the Protocol only shields a move when both sides are members at the moment you leave. A stale assumption here is the most expensive mistake in a breakaway.

The Protocol for Broker Recruiting was meant to make advisor moves cleaner by removing litigation over a small set of client data. That promise only holds between two member firms. Because the membership roster changes, the verification step is not a formality you can skip based on what was true a year ago.

## Membership is a moving target

The Protocol began with three firms and grew to well over a thousand signatories, but the direction has not been one way. Some large firms have withdrawn, with Morgan Stanley exiting in 2017 and UBS in 2018 among the most cited examples, and smaller firms join and leave on their own schedules. The roster you check today is not guaranteed to match the roster on your resignation date.

This is why "my firm is a Protocol member" is not a fact you can carry from memory. It is a status you confirm close to your departure, document, and re-confirm if your timeline slips. The cost of getting it wrong is not a paperwork delay. It is losing the litigation shield for the entire move and exposing yourself to a misappropriation or non-solicit claim.

## How to verify membership before you resign

Verification has two halves, and most advisors only think about one.

| Step | What you are confirming |
|---|---|
| Confirm the departing firm is a current member | That the Protocol shield exists for your move at all |
| Confirm your new entity is a signatory before resignation | That you are eligible to receive Protocol protection |
| Document both confirmations with a date | That you can prove member status as of your resignation day |
| Re-confirm if your timeline shifts | That a withdrawal between planning and resigning did not void your assumption |

The point worth repeating: this is a two-sided check, and both sides have to be true on the same day.

## The timing trap with early registration

Here is the tension nobody warns you about. Your new RIA entity must be a Protocol signatory before you resign. But standing up and registering that entity creates a paper trail, and registering too early can signal your departure to the firm you are leaving before you are ready for them to know.

Move too late and you resign without Protocol protection in place. Move too early and you tip off the firm, which can trigger a faster, harder response and shorten your runway. The window is real and it is narrow. Plan the sequence with your attorney and your custodian so registration, signatory status, and resignation land in the right order rather than racing each other.

## What changes if either firm is not a member

If verification comes back showing the departing firm has withdrawn, you are running a non-protocol move, and the document workflow changes more than people expect. In a Protocol move, you carry the five permitted client fields and stage repapering around them. In a non-protocol move, you carry no firm-sourced data and rebuild from client-initiated contact and documents the client provides directly.

That shift puts more weight on document automation that works from client-supplied paperwork rather than firm exports. ## A pre-resignation membership sequence

1. Confirm the departing firm's current Protocol status and save dated evidence.
2. Form your new RIA and make it a signatory, timed to avoid early signaling.
3. Re-confirm both statuses close to your resignation date if any time has passed.
4. If either firm is not a member, switch to the non-protocol workflow and engage counsel immediately.
5. Stage document automation to work from client-supplied paperwork so you are clean either way.

The advisors who get this right treat membership as a live status, not a settled fact, and they sequence registration and resignation deliberately. The ones who get burned assumed last year's roster still held on resignation day.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is Broker Protocol membership permanent once a firm joins?**
No. Firms join and withdraw over time, and several large firms have left, including Morgan Stanley in 2017 and UBS in 2018. Because the roster changes, you must confirm current membership for both firms close to your resignation date rather than relying on past status.

**How do I confirm both firms are Broker Protocol members?**
Confirm the firm you are leaving is a current member and that your new entity is a signatory before you resign, then document both with a date. If your timeline slips, re-confirm, because a withdrawal between planning and resigning would void the protection you were counting on.

**Why is registering my new RIA early a risk?**
Your new entity must be a Protocol signatory before you resign, but registering it creates a paper trail that can signal your departure to your current firm prematurely. Too early tips them off; too late leaves you unprotected. The sequence needs deliberate planning with your attorney and custodian.

**How does a non-protocol move change my document workflow?**
You stop carrying firm-sourced client data and rebuild from client-initiated contact and documents clients provide directly. That puts more weight on document automation that extracts details from client-supplied paperwork to pre-fill forms and cut NIGO, keeping you on the safe side of the data ownership line.
