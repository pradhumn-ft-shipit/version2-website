# Realistic Timeline: Moving a $250M Wirehouse Book to a New RIA in 6 Months

The "six-month transition" is a planning frame, not a literal six-month execution window. For a $250M wirehouse book, the actual on-platform work — from resignation to operational completion — runs 21–45 days. The other five months are about preparation: choosing the new firm, building the operational stack, getting comfortable with the regulatory and tax mechanics, and pre-staging the resignation event itself.

Advisors who try to compress the six months into one or two months almost always make preventable mistakes that cost them clients. Advisors who extend the six months into a year usually lose momentum and never resign. Six months is the right planning horizon because it forces deliberateness without letting urgency dissipate.

This is what each month of the six-month timeline should look like, what gets done, what gets decided, and what the failure modes are at each stage.

## Month 1: deciding to leave (and choosing where to go)

The first month is the highest-stakes month and the one most advisors underinvest in. The decision to leave is partly emotional, partly economic, partly philosophical about the business model — and rushing past any of those dimensions creates regret 18 months later.

The economic decision is the most quantifiable. The framework: take your current wirehouse payout (typically 35–45% on advisory revenue net of all deductions), compare to the all-in RIA economics (typically 65–80% net after platform fees, custody, technology, and overhead), and apply a present value calculation over a 10-year horizon. For a $250M book at 0.8% fees, that's $2M in annual revenue. The payout differential alone is typically $400K–$700K per year, depending on the wirehouse's deferred comp structure.

But the economic decision has hidden complexity. Deferred compensation is usually forfeited on exit. Recruiting bonuses from years past can have callback clauses. The advisor's effective payout at the wirehouse is often higher than nominal because of bonus and recruiting comp that doesn't show up on the standard payout grid. The honest comparison requires modeling 7-year forward economics at both firms.

The firm-selection decision involves at least three serious due diligence visits. The advisor visits the new firm, meets the operations team, talks to a recently-transitioned advisor at the firm, and gets specific commitments on transition timeline, technology stack, compliance support, and client communication infrastructure. A firm that can't commit to a 30-day transition is one that hasn't done the operational work to deliver it.

Month 1 deliverable: a signed agreement (in principle) with the new firm and a written economic model justifying the move.

## Month 2: building the personal data infrastructure

Month 2 is when the advisor builds the personal infrastructure they'll need to operate independently from their wirehouse data. This is entirely pre-resignation work, and it's the foundation for everything that happens after resignation.

The first task is building a client contact database from public sources. The advisor cannot legally copy any data from the wirehouse's CRM. But they can rebuild a contact database from LinkedIn, personal phone records, holiday card lists, family relationships they remember, and their own personal email archive. For most advisors, this is 6–10 hours of focused work over two weeks. The output is a spreadsheet with name, contact info, household composition, and the advisor's notes on each client relationship.

The second task is documenting the book by memory. The advisor writes down — without referencing wirehouse systems — the rough AUM, account types, primary investment approach, and known life situations for each client. This is what the new firm will use as the basis for the transition strategy and what the advisor will use for prioritization after resignation.

The third task is mapping the book to the new firm's custodial relationships. Which clients will go to Schwab, which to [Fidelity](https://www.fidelity.com/wealth-services/working-with-fidelity), which to [Pershing](https://www.pershing.com)? Are there account types (529 plans, annuities, alternative investments) that need special handling at the new firm? Each of these decisions affects the timeline and the operational complexity of the transition.

Month 2 deliverable: a complete client contact database, a memory-based book summary, and a custodial assignment plan.

## Month 3: legal and regulatory preparation

Month 3 is the legal-heavy month. The advisor engages with a securities attorney experienced in advisor transitions and works through the regulatory landscape.

The key documents reviewed: the advisor's current employment agreement (for non-compete, non-solicit, and deferred comp clauses), the Broker Protocol status of both the old and new firms (the rules are different if either firm is not Protocol-compliant), the FINRA Form U4 transfer process and any Form U5 disclosure implications, and the state-by-state registration requirements for the advisor's client geography. The [FINRA books and records guidance](https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/key-topics/books-records) covers what records the new firm needs to maintain from day one.

The most contentious legal question in most transitions is the non-solicit clause. The Broker Protocol historically gave advisors safe harbor to contact clients about a move within specific guidelines. Some wirehouses have exited Protocol — meaning the advisor's old employment contract governs, and aggressive enforcement of non-solicits is possible. This decision shapes the entire client communication strategy and is best resolved with attorney advice before resignation.

The U4 transfer mechanics matter because they determine how quickly the advisor can legally operate at the new firm. Modern transition platforms file U4 transfers within 2 hours of resignation, with most states clearing within 8–12 hours. The advisor should know the state-by-state timeline for their client geography because the slowest state determines when the advisor is fully operational.

Month 3 deliverable: legal opinion letter on the transition path, FINRA filing plan with timing, and state registration sequencing.

## Month 4: pre-staging operational infrastructure

Month 4 is when the new firm's operations team and the advisor work together to pre-stage everything that can be pre-staged.

The new firm's CRM gets provisioned. The advisor's email and tooling get provisioned. The client communication templates get drafted, voice-matched to the advisor, and reviewed by compliance. The custodial liaison at the new firm gets briefed on the incoming book. The compliance review of the communication plan gets completed.

This is also when transition technology platforms get configured for the specific book. The AI-driven form generation system gets loaded with the advisor's client database (the public-source version from month 2), normalized into the new firm's master schema, and used to generate dry-run versions of the custodial forms each client will need to sign. This dry-run process surfaces data gaps before resignation, when there's still time to fix them through advisor memory and public-record validation.

The most common month 4 mistake is treating this work as the new firm's responsibility alone. The advisor has to be deeply engaged — confirming custodial assignments, reviewing communication templates, validating the client database, and making decisions about how to handle complex accounts. Advisors who delegate month 4 entirely to the new firm end up with worse transitions because the operational team is missing book-specific knowledge.

Month 4 deliverable: fully provisioned new-firm infrastructure, dry-run client form library, voice-matched communication templates, and a transition runbook with named owners for every step.

## Month 5: dress rehearsal and final preparation

Month 5 is when the firm and the advisor run a complete dress rehearsal of the transition without actually executing it.

The dress rehearsal includes: simulated resignation, simulated U4 filing, simulated client communication launch, simulated first 48 hours of operations. The point is to expose the operational gaps before they matter. Where does the runbook break down? Which client communication templates need revision? What happens if a custodial liaison contact is unavailable at the critical moment?

The dress rehearsal also includes a financial check. The advisor confirms their personal financial runway to handle 30–60 days of reduced cash flow during the transition window. Most advisors underestimate this: the wirehouse will typically claw back recent commissions, withhold final paychecks, and dispute deferred comp. The advisor should plan for 60 days of zero income from the wirehouse and a slow ramp of income at the new firm.

The final preparation step is the resignation date selection. The optimal day is typically a Friday, ideally after the advisor's bonus accrual cutoff, ideally during a market-quiet period, ideally before a long weekend (because the dark period spans fewer market days). The exact date is selected based on these factors plus the new firm's operational availability.

Month 5 deliverable: completed dress rehearsal, identified operational gaps closed, resignation date set, financial runway confirmed.

## Month 6: resignation, transition, and stabilization

Month 6 is the execution month. The actual transition work — what most advisors think of when they think about transitions — happens in the first 21–45 days of month 6.

Days 1–3 (the dark period): resignation, U4 transfer, state registration clearance, and first client contact via the pre-staged communication queue. Target: 24-hour dark period, sub-5% NIGO rate on initial form submissions, 100% first-contact completion within 24 hours of legal clearance.

Days 4–21 (active transition): client signatures collected, custodial forms submitted, exception handling on the 5–10% of accounts that have complications (trust structures, unusual account types, regulatory restrictions). Target: 95% of accounts in submission by day 21.

Days 22–45 (stabilization): final account confirmations, ACATS completions, [client onboarding](/solutions/client-onboarding) to new-firm tools and communications, and the first cycle of normal practice management at the new firm. Target: 100% of viable accounts confirmed at the new firm by day 45.

The remaining 15 days of month 6 are reserved for post-transition cleanup: handling stragglers, completing the audit trail, resolving any compliance follow-ups, and beginning the practice management work at the new firm. The advisor's goal at the end of month 6 is to be operating normally — fielding client questions, doing planning meetings, managing portfolios — not still chasing transition paperwork.

Month 6 deliverable: 95%+ AUM retention, fully transferred book at the new firm, completed compliance audit trail, and operational stabilization.

## Where transitions actually fail

Six-month timelines fail most often in the same three ways.

**Failure one: under-investment in month 2.** Advisors rush through the personal data infrastructure work and end up with incomplete client databases that produce NIGO-heavy form submissions. The cost shows up in month 6 as 20%+ NIGO rates and a 60-day transition that should have been 30 days.

**Failure two: under-investment in month 4.** Advisors delegate pre-staging entirely to the new firm and discover too late that the runbook has gaps. The cost shows up as operational confusion in the first 48 hours of month 6 — exactly when the dark period is most dangerous.

**Failure three: over-rushing month 6 execution.** Advisors who try to compress the transition into 14 days instead of 21–45 days end up with worse client communication, more NIGOs, and lower AUM retention. The "more is better" instinct on speed produces diminishing returns past a certain compression point.

The right framing of the six-month timeline isn't "make it shorter." It's "make each month do what it's supposed to do" — and that's what produces 95%+ AUM retention and a clean handoff to the new firm.

For advisors evaluating whether they're ready to start a six-month process, the most useful diagnostic is whether they can describe in detail what each of the six months will involve. If the answer is fuzzy past month 1 or 2, the right move is to spend another 30 days on planning before committing to the timeline.

## Frequently asked questions

**Why does a $250M transition take six months when the actual work happens in 21-45 days?**

The first five months are preparation: firm selection, legal review, personal data infrastructure, operational pre-staging, and dress rehearsal. Compressing this preparation produces measurably worse transition outcomes — typically lower AUM retention and higher NIGO rates. The actual on-platform work is short; the preparation determines whether that short window goes well.

**What's the realistic AUM retention rate for a 6-month planned transition of a $250M book?**

With proper six-month preparation and modern [transition automation](/solutions/advisor-transitions), 95-98% AUM retention is achievable. Rushed transitions (under 60 days of preparation) average 88-92% retention. The 5-6 percentage point retention difference is roughly $12-15M in retained AUM on a $250M book, dwarfing the cost of careful preparation.

**Should the advisor tell anyone at the wirehouse during the six-month preparation window?**

No. The advisor should treat the preparation as confidential through the resignation date. Any disclosure to a wirehouse colleague — even one perceived as trustworthy — creates substantial risk that the wirehouse will respond preemptively, including the possibility of garden leave, deferred comp claw-backs, or legal action that complicates the transition.

**What's the optimal day of the week to resign for a wirehouse-to-RIA transition?**

Friday afternoon is most common because it minimizes the wirehouse's same-day retention activity and lets the advisor use the weekend for first client outreach without market activity competing for attention. Some advisors choose Thursday to get an extra business day of state registration progress, but the trade-off is exposure to wirehouse retention calls on Friday.

**How much financial runway does the advisor need during the transition window?**

Plan for 60 days of zero income from the wirehouse and a slow income ramp at the new firm. Most advisors find they're back to normal cash flow by day 90 post-resignation. The actual runway depends on personal expenses and the wirehouse's specific clawback and deferred comp practices, but 60-90 days is a reasonable planning baseline.

**Can the six-month timeline be compressed for a smaller book (under $100M)?**

Modestly, yes. A $50M book can be transitioned with three months of preparation rather than six, primarily because the operational complexity is lower (fewer accounts, fewer custodial relationships, fewer regulatory considerations). The legal and firm-selection work is comparable regardless of book size, so the compression comes from the operational pre-staging phases.
