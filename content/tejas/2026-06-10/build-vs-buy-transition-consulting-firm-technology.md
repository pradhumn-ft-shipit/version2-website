# Build vs Buy: Should Your Transition Consulting Firm Build Its Own Tech?

The build-versus-buy question hits every transition consulting firm at the same inflection point: somewhere between $5M and $15M in annual revenue, when the firm has enough scale to consider technology investment but not yet enough to make the wrong choice without consequence. Build the wrong thing, and you've sunk 18 months and $2M into something the market doesn't want. Buy the wrong platform, and you've locked your operations into a vendor whose roadmap doesn't match your strategy.

The instinct most consulting firm partners have is to build. There's a strong logic to it: your firm's value proposition is the operational expertise you've built over years of running transitions. If you can productize that expertise, you turn services revenue into software revenue with much better margins. Industry stories about successful tech-enabled services firms reinforce this instinct. Build seems like the path to a multi-billion-dollar firm; buy seems like the path to remaining a services business indefinitely.

The honest answer is more complicated. Build makes sense for a small fraction of consulting firms — those with very specific market positioning and the operational discipline to be a software company while still running services. Buy makes sense for everyone else. This is the framework for figuring out which group you're in.

## The build case: when it actually works

Building proprietary transition technology can work, but the firms it works for share four characteristics that are rarer than they look.

**Characteristic 1: a highly specific operational niche.** The firms that successfully build proprietary technology typically have a very specific niche — a sub-segment of transitions that's underserved by general platforms. Examples: ultra-high-net-worth private wealth transitions with custom legal entity structures, cross-border transitions for international advisors, or transitions involving specific custodial relationships not served by mainstream platforms. The niche has to be specific enough that off-the-shelf platforms don't serve it well and large enough to support a software business.

**Characteristic 2: enough recurring transitions to validate at scale.** Building software requires real-world testing at meaningful volume. Firms that successfully build typically have 50+ transitions per year of their own — enough to validate the platform with real data, expose edge cases, and iterate quickly. Firms doing 10–15 transitions per year don't have enough internal volume to develop a robust platform; they end up with software that breaks on the second customer.

**Characteristic 3: capital and runway for an 18-30 month build.** Real platform development takes 18–30 months from kickoff to production-ready software. The capital requirement is typically $2–5M depending on scope. Firms that try to build on less capital or shorter timelines produce thin software that doesn't compete with mature platforms.

**Characteristic 4: software-company DNA.** The hardest characteristic to acquire. Running a software business requires different operational disciplines than running a services business — product management, engineering hiring, customer success, technical support, security and compliance posture, ongoing platform evolution. Many consulting firms underestimate how much of their leadership attention will be required by the software side of the business.

If your firm has all four characteristics, building can produce a defensible, high-margin business. If your firm has only two or three, the build is much riskier than it appears.

## The buy case: when partnership beats ownership

The buy case is the right answer for most transition consulting firms — but not because building is impossible. It's because the strategic positioning a firm gets from buying is often better than the positioning it would have gotten from building.

**Argument 1: speed to operational improvement.** Buying a mature platform produces operational improvement in 60–90 days. Building produces operational improvement in 18–30 months — during which time your competitors who bought are taking your customers with better service. The opportunity cost of the build window is usually larger than the strategic value of ownership.

**Argument 2: leverage on someone else's roadmap.** A mature transition platform invests $10M+ per year in R&D, with a dedicated product team, customer feedback loops across hundreds of customers, and the engineering resources to chase emerging requirements (new custodial integrations, new compliance frameworks, new AI capabilities). Your firm cannot match that R&D investment internally. Buying means you ride on top of it.

**Argument 3: focus on your differentiator.** Your firm's competitive advantage is operational expertise — the judgment to handle complex transitions, the relationships to navigate broker-dealer and custodial channels, the trust your clients have built with your team. Software is not your differentiator. Buying lets you keep your investment focused on what differentiates you, while leveraging vendor investment for the commodity layer.

**Argument 4: lower risk of strategic distraction.** Many consulting firms that started building proprietary technology have ended up spending more leadership time on the software side than the services side — to the detriment of both. The buy path keeps your leadership focused on the services business while letting the vendor handle the technology evolution.

The dominant strategic logic for buy is that it's the path that lets the consulting firm grow faster without becoming something it doesn't have the DNA to become.

## The hybrid model: partner-plus-customization

A third path that more consulting firms are taking: partner with a vendor for the core platform, customize meaningfully on top. This hybrid model captures most of the benefits of both build and buy.

The hybrid model works like this. The firm partners with a transition platform vendor for the core operational layer — form generation, custodial submission, data validation, audit trails. The firm builds a customization layer on top: proprietary workflows, branded client experiences, custom analytics, and integrations with the firm's specific tooling. The customization layer is where the firm's operational expertise shows up; the core platform handles the commodity work.

This hybrid model is significantly cheaper than building from scratch — typically 10–20% of the cost — and produces results in 60–120 days rather than 18–30 months. The trade-off is that the firm doesn't own the underlying platform IP, which limits the long-term strategic optionality. For most consulting firms, this trade-off is well worth taking.

The vendors who support the hybrid model well offer flexible APIs, white-label options, and partner programs designed to let consulting firms operate as platform extensions rather than as customers. The vendors who don't support the hybrid model well are usually the ones who push back against customization or require expensive integration work for every modification.

## The capital math: build versus buy at scale

A useful exercise for any consulting firm partner considering build-versus-buy is the explicit capital math over a 5-year horizon.

**Build path, year-by-year:**

Year 1: $1.2M development cost (engineering team of 4–6, product manager, technical infrastructure). $0 revenue from platform.
Year 2: $1.5M development plus first deployment costs. Revenue from internal use only; no external customers.
Year 3: $1.8M including initial sales/marketing and customer success. First 2–3 external customers if positioning is favorable.
Year 4: $2.2M including expanded team. Revenue ramping to $1M–$2M if execution is on track.
Year 5: $2.5M operational. Revenue at $2M–$5M if everything has gone well.

Five-year total investment: $9.2M. Five-year revenue: $3M–$7M cumulative. The firm is still in investment mode by year 5 if it's building a real software business.

**Buy path, year-by-year:**

Year 1: $200K–$500K platform licensing and implementation. Immediate operational improvement.
Year 2: $300K–$700K ongoing licensing. Operational efficiency gains continuing to compound.
Year 3: $400K–$900K. Firm has scaled transition volume on the platform; [pricing](/pricing) reflects throughput.
Year 4: $500K–$1.1M. The firm is using platform capabilities to handle larger and more complex transitions.
Year 5: $600K–$1.3M. Mature operational model with consistent throughput.

Five-year total cost: $2M–$4.5M. The savings versus build: $4.7M–$7.2M. That savings can fund organic growth, M&A, or recruiting in ways the build path can't.

For most consulting firms, the buy-path capital math wins decisively. The firms where build wins are the ones that successfully transition into being software companies — which is a small percentage.

## The decision framework

The decision framework that produces the right answer for most consulting firms is straightforward.

**Question 1: Are we strategically committed to becoming a software company?** If the answer is "no, we're a services company that uses technology" — buy. If the answer is "yes, our strategic future is as a software-and-services hybrid" — consider build, but require the four characteristics from earlier in the article.

**Question 2: Do we have $5M–$10M of capital we can lose without threatening the firm?** Building involves real risk of capital loss. If the answer is no, buy. If yes, build is feasible but still has to clear other criteria.

**Question 3: Are we operating in a niche that existing platforms don't serve well?** If your transitions look broadly like other firms' transitions, off-the-shelf platforms will serve you well. If your transitions have unusual characteristics (cross-border, ultra-HNW, specific regulatory frameworks) that mainstream platforms don't address — that's a build signal.

**Question 4: Can our leadership team realistically run both a services business and a software business?** This is the hardest question. Many firms underestimate the leadership attention required for software. If the leadership team is already at capacity running the services business, adding software is likely to degrade both.

If you answer yes to all four questions, build is a reasonable path. If you answer no to any of them, buy is the right answer.

## What the build case looks like when it works

To make the build case concrete: the firms that have successfully built transition technology in the past 5 years share a specific pattern.

They started with a very specific operational niche — typically ultra-high-net-worth or cross-border transitions where the operational requirements are non-standard. They had 100+ transitions per year of their own volume before they started building. They funded the build with $5M+ in committed capital. They hired a dedicated CTO and engineering team rather than trying to build with consultants. They treated the build as a 24-month commitment, not a 9-month sprint. They positioned the eventual software product to serve their existing customer base first, then expanded to adjacent markets.

This pattern is not common. The firms that follow it produce real software businesses. The firms that try a lighter version of this pattern produce software that doesn't compete and ends up shelved.

For most consulting firms, the honest answer is buy, with the option of moving to hybrid as the operational model matures and the firm's strategic positioning becomes clearer. That path produces the best risk-adjusted outcome for the vast majority of firms in the build-versus-buy decision.

## Frequently asked questions

**What's the typical capital requirement to build a competitive transition platform from scratch?**

$5-10M over a 24-30 month development timeline, including engineering staff, product management, infrastructure, security and compliance work, and initial customer success. Firms that attempt to build for less than $5M typically produce thin software that doesn't compete with mature platforms in the market.

**Can a consulting firm partner with a vendor and white-label the platform?**

Yes, this is the hybrid model and it's increasingly common. Vendors offering white-label and partner programs typically allow the consulting firm to brand the customer-facing experience while leveraging the vendor's core platform. The commercial structures vary — some are revenue-share, some are flat licensing, some are tiered based on transition volume.

**How long does it take to see operational improvement from buying versus building?**

Buy path: 60-90 days from contract signature to operational improvement. Build path: 18-30 months to a first version that meaningfully improves operations. The build path's delay during the development window represents real opportunity cost — competitors using off-the-shelf platforms are improving faster during that window.

**What's the most common mistake consulting firms make in build-versus-buy decisions?**

Underestimating the leadership attention required to build and run software. Many firms assume the build can happen in the background while leadership stays focused on services. In practice, building software requires substantial CEO and partner attention for at least 18 months — and that attention has to come from somewhere, usually at the cost of services growth.

**Is there a transition volume below which building is clearly the wrong answer?**

Yes. Firms doing fewer than 50 transitions per year of their own volume cannot adequately validate a platform during development. Without real-world testing at scale, the resulting platform breaks on customer two and three. Below 50 transitions per year, buy is essentially the only viable answer.

**Does building proprietary technology create a defensible competitive moat for a consulting firm?**

It can, but only when paired with a specific market niche the platform serves better than alternatives. Generic transition technology doesn't create a moat because the firm is competing with vendors who have 10x the engineering resources. Niche-specific platforms can create defensible positioning — but the niche has to be real and underserved.
