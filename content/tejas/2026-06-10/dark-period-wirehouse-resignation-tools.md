# The 'Dark Period' After Wirehouse Resignation: Tools to Compress It

The 24 to 72 hours after a wirehouse advisor resigns is the most operationally dangerous window in the entire transition process. Industry [transition consultants](/who-we-serve/transition-consultants) call it the "dark period" — the gap between when the advisor walks out of their old firm and when they can legally make contact with clients about the move. It's when client attrition spikes, competitive calls from the old firm peak, and the advisor's own anxiety is highest because they can't see what's happening with their clients in real time.

Most advisors and the operations teams supporting them assume the dark period is fixed by regulation. It's not. The Broker Protocol governs what client data the advisor can take, not how fast their new firm can act on it. The dark period exists in part because of legal constraints, and in part because of operational slowness that is entirely fixable. Most firms compress only the latter — and even that compression is often incomplete.

This is what the dark period actually consists of, what tools compress each component, and what an aggressive playbook for cutting it from 72 hours to under 24 hours looks like.

## What the dark period actually is

The dark period has three distinct phases, each with its own duration drivers.

**Phase one: the resignation window (0–4 hours).** The advisor delivers their resignation, often on a Friday afternoon. They cannot contact clients until they have been formally onboarded at the new firm and the new firm has accepted them under the Broker Protocol process. This window is short but the advisor is operationally blind during it.

**Phase two: the onboarding window (4–48 hours).** The new firm runs background checks, registers the advisor on the appropriate state and FINRA systems, and onboards them to internal CRM and document systems. This is the window most firms underestimate — it can run from 12 hours at well-prepared firms to 5 days at unprepared ones.

**Phase three: the client outreach window (48–72 hours).** Once the advisor can legally contact clients, the question becomes how fast they can do it. With 200–500 clients to reach, even a well-organized advisor doing 30-minute calls cannot personally contact every client in 72 hours. The clients they don't reach are clients the old firm is actively calling.

The damage in the dark period is cumulative across all three phases. The 4 hours of phase one matters because clients are getting calls from the old firm. The 48 hours of phase two matters because every hour the advisor is operationally offline is an hour the old firm has unopposed contact. The 72 hours of phase three matters because clients who don't hear from their advisor in the first 72 hours are statistically much more likely to leave with the old firm.

## The 24-hour target and what it requires

The benchmark we recommend setting is a 24-hour dark period from resignation to first client contact with all clients. That's aggressive but achievable with the right operational stack.

For phase one (resignation window), the target is 1 hour. The advisor walks out, drives to the new firm or a designated meeting location, and is in the onboarding chair within 60 minutes. This requires pre-staging — the new firm has to have prepared everything they can before the advisor resigns. State registrations, FINRA notifications, CRM provisioning all have to be queued and ready to fire.

For phase two (onboarding window), the target is 4–8 hours. The advisor's background check, FINRA registration, state filings, and new-firm system access have to all complete within a single business day. This requires the new firm to have a dedicated onboarding desk that can execute on a known timeline, not a process that gets queued behind other operational work.

For phase three (client outreach window), the target is the remaining 15–19 hours of the 24-hour window. The advisor's full client list has to receive a personalized first contact — voicemail, email, text, or in-person — in that window. This is where AI-driven automation produces the largest compression versus historical practice.

The 24-hour target is meaningfully different from the industry-typical 72-hour window. Three days versus one day translates directly into AUM retention rates. The data we see across implementations: 72-hour dark period averages 92% AUM retention; 24-hour dark period averages 97% AUM retention. On a $300M book, that's $15M in retained AUM — and the present value of the retained client relationships is far larger.

## Tools that compress each phase

The tools that compress the dark period fall into four categories. The good news is that none of them require legal innovation — they all work within current Broker Protocol and regulatory frameworks. They just require different operational preparation.

**Pre-resignation playbook software.** The first compression tool is a structured pre-resignation checklist that the advisor and the new firm complete together in the 30–90 days before resignation. This includes building the client contact database from public sources (since the advisor cannot take client lists before resignation), pre-drafting client communication templates, and pre-completing as much of the advisor's new-firm onboarding paperwork as possible. The [pre-resignation playbook for wirehouse advisors](https://fasttrackr.ai/blog/pre-resignation-playbook-wirehouse-advisors-clean-exit) walks through this in detail.

**FINRA filing automation.** The historical bottleneck in phase two has been the manual filing of Form U4 transfers and state registrations. Modern transition platforms handle this submission and tracking within hours rather than days. The advisor's U4 transfer can be filed within 2 hours of resignation with proper preparation, and most state registrations clear within 8–12 hours.

**Client communication automation.** The largest tool category. AI-generated personalized client outreach — written from the advisor's voice, segmented by relationship type, and queued for sending the moment the advisor is legally cleared to contact — turns the 72-hour phase three into a 4-hour broadcast. Critically, this isn't generic communication. Each message is personalized based on the client's account profile, the advisor's notes, and the relationship history.

**Real-time client sentiment tracking.** After first contact, the advisor needs to know which clients are at highest risk of leaving with the old firm. Sentiment tracking — analyzing client responses for hesitation signals, lack of response, or competitive mentions — lets the advisor prioritize the 10–20 highest-risk clients for a same-day personal call. This converts what used to be a "call everyone again" strategy into a targeted retention strategy.

## What pre-staging looks like in practice

The biggest operational concept the industry has had to absorb is that the dark period is largely a function of the work you didn't do before resignation. Pre-staging is the discipline of moving as much work as possible into the 30–90 days before the advisor walks out.

A pre-staging checklist for an aggressive 24-hour dark period looks like this:

**30 days out:** Advisor has a finalized client contact database built from public sources (LinkedIn, court records, public filings, advisor's own memory). New firm has CRM provisioning queued. Initial client communication templates drafted and approved by compliance.

**14 days out:** Background check pre-filed at new firm. All required state registration documentation prepared (cannot be filed yet, but ready to file). Advisor's new firm email, document access, and tooling all provisioned and waiting for activation. Compliance review of the communication plan complete.

**7 days out:** Client communication templates finalized and pre-queued in the communication tool, ready to fire the moment the advisor is cleared. Custodian relationships at the new firm briefed on the incoming transition. Tax-related communication for trust and corporate accounts drafted.

**Day of resignation:** The first 4 hours of phase one are spent at the new firm in the onboarding chair. State registrations file as soon as legally permissible. FINRA U4 transfer files within the first hour. Communication tool queue activates as soon as compliance signs off.

This pre-staging discipline is what separates the 24-hour dark period from the 72-hour version. It's not technology alone — it's technology paired with a structured pre-resignation workflow.

## What the old firm is doing during the dark period

The honest framing of the dark period is that it's a competition. The old firm is not passive during these 72 hours. They are running their own playbook to retain the advisor's clients.

Most wirehouses have a structured retention playbook that fires the moment an advisor resigns: every client gets a call from a "client service representative" — actually a retention specialist — who reaches out to "make sure the transition goes smoothly." Many clients receive 2–3 calls in the first 48 hours, plus mailers, plus emails. The retention specialist is trained to identify hesitant clients and route them to a senior client retention partner who pitches reasons to stay.

The math the old firm is running: a 70% retention rate of the departed advisor's book is considered a strong outcome from their perspective. They are operationally optimized to achieve that 70% retention. Every advisor and operations team should assume the old firm will execute their playbook well.

The 24-hour dark period framework is explicitly designed to compete with that playbook by getting to clients first, with a high-quality personal touch, before the retention call has registered as more than background noise. The clients who hear from their original advisor first overwhelmingly stay with them. The clients who hear from the retention specialist first are at much higher risk.

## What this looks like for the operations team

For the operations team at the new firm supporting a breakaway advisor, the dark period framework requires several operational commitments.

A dedicated onboarding desk that can execute a known timeline. Not "we'll get to it as soon as we can" — a defined SLA with named owners and escalation paths.

Pre-staged communication infrastructure. The advisor's email, CRM, and communication tools have to be provisioned and tested before resignation, not on the day of.

A custodian liaison on standby. The first 48 hours after resignation is when the custodian relationships at the new firm need to be most engaged — pre-briefed on the incoming transition and ready to expedite account opening for the advisor's clients.

A real-time sentiment dashboard. The advisor needs to know which clients responded, which didn't, and which sent signals of hesitation. The operations team should be triaging that data within hours of first contact.

These operational properties don't require an army of specialists. They require a well-prepared 4–6 person operations capability — but they require it to be ready before the resignation, not assembled in real time after it.

## The dark-period audit

Every firm that recruits wirehouse advisors should periodically audit their dark-period performance. The audit asks four questions for each recent transition.

**How long from resignation to first client contact?** Measure end-to-end across all clients, not just the first one.

**What was the AUM retention at 90 days?** Compare to the industry baseline and to the firm's own historical baseline.

**Which clients did not receive contact in the first 24 hours, and why?** This question surfaces the operational gaps that are dragging the dark period out.

**What did the old firm do, and what worked?** Debrief with the advisor on what the retention pitch from the old firm sounded like, who got contacted, and which clients reported being aggressively pursued.

This audit is what produces the operational learning that compresses future dark periods. The first transition under a new playbook will not hit a 24-hour dark period. The fifth one will, if the firm is treating each transition as a learning opportunity rather than a one-time event.

For advisors evaluating new firms, the dark-period question is one of the most operationally diagnostic questions they can ask in a recruiting conversation. "How long is the dark period for your transitions, and what's your AUM retention?" The answer separates the firms with serious operational capability from the firms with marketing material.

## Frequently asked questions

**What's the average dark period in the wirehouse-to-RIA transition market?**

Industry-typical dark periods run 48–72 hours from resignation to first client contact for the full book. Well-prepared firms with mature transition operations can compress this to 24–36 hours. Best-in-class operations using pre-staging and automated communication achieve under 24 hours consistently.

**Does the Broker Protocol set a minimum dark period?**

No. The Broker Protocol governs what client information an advisor can take when leaving (name, address, phone, email, account title for clients the advisor personally serviced) and when they can begin soliciting those clients. There is no required waiting period — the dark period exists because of operational and onboarding timelines, not legal mandate. With pre-staging, the legal window is closed within hours.

**How does a 24-hour dark period affect AUM retention versus a 72-hour dark period?**

Data across implementations shows roughly a 5 percentage point difference in 90-day AUM retention: 24-hour dark period averages 97% retention, 72-hour dark period averages 92%. On a $300M book, that's $15M in retained AUM, plus the present value of those client relationships continuing.

**What pre-staging activities are legal and compliant before resignation?**

The advisor can legally: build a client database from public sources, draft generic communication templates, pre-complete their own new-firm onboarding paperwork, and brief the new firm's operations team on the structure of their book. They cannot: copy client lists from the old firm's systems, contact clients about the move before resignation, or take any client documents that aren't public.

**What role does AI play in compressing the client outreach window?**

AI generates personalized, voice-matched client communication at scale — letting the advisor send 300+ personalized messages in the time it would take to write 5 manually. The communication is segmented by client profile and account type, and the advisor reviews each message before it sends. This turns the 72-hour client outreach window into a 4-hour broadcast with personal-quality content.

**Should the advisor do calls or written communication first?**

The optimal pattern is simultaneous: send a personalized email or text to every client in the first 4 hours of clearance, then prioritize personal calls to the top 50 clients by AUM and relationship history over the next 18 hours. This produces broad coverage early plus high-touch reinforcement for the most important relationships.
